# Fecal Immunochemical Test (FIT): At-Home Colon Cancer Screening Made Easy

## What FIT detects and how it works

The FIT test uses antibodies that specifically bind human hemoglobin to identify microscopic blood in a stool sample. This targeted approach improves specificity compared with older guaiac-based stool tests that can react to non-human blood or certain foods and medications. FIT does not generally require dietary restrictions prior to sample collection, which reduces pre-test preparation and potential false positives related to diet.

## Advantages of an at-home FIT

- Convenience: A small, self-collected stool sample can be obtained at home and mailed to a laboratory for analysis.  
- Non-invasive: No sedation, bowel preparation, or in-clinic procedure is required for the screening itself.  
- Sensitivity for bleeding lesions: FIT is sensitive to blood from lesions in the lower gastrointestinal tract and can be used as part of population screening strategies.

## How to complete the test

Most FIT kits include clear instructions and a collection device (often a small brush or stick) to transfer a tiny stool sample into a sealed tube. After sealing the sample, follow the kit instructions for mailing or returning it to the lab. Results are typically available within one to two weeks, depending on the lab.

## Who should consider FIT screening

Clinical guidelines commonly recommend colorectal cancer screening beginning at age 45 for average-risk adults, with individualized timing for those with family history or other risk factors. FIT can be offered annually or biennially depending on guideline preference and risk assessment; persistent or recurrent positive results should prompt diagnostic colonoscopy.

## Interpreting results

- Negative result: No detectable blood in the tested sample. Regular re-screening as recommended remains important because tests represent a point-in-time assessment.  
- Positive result: Blood was detected. A positive FIT does not prove cancer; it signals the need for further diagnostic evaluation, most often colonoscopy, to identify the source of bleeding.

If a positive result occurs, timely follow-up is advised to determine the cause and initiate appropriate management.
