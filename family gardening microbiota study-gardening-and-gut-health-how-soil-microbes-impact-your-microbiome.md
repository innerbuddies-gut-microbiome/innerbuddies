# Gardening and Gut Health: How Soil Microbes Impact Your Microbiome

Gardening is more than plant care and fresh food—it also represents sustained contact with the complex microbial communities in soil. Recent observational studies comparing gardeners with non-gardeners suggest that routine exposure to soil can introduce environmental microbes into human microbial communities, with potential implications for digestion, immune function, and mental health.

## The gut microbiome and environmental microbes

The gut microbiome consists of trillions of bacteria, fungi, viruses and other microorganisms that interact closely with host physiology. A diverse and balanced microbiome supports nutrient metabolism, trains the immune system, and contributes to the gut–brain axis that influences mood and stress resilience. Environmental sources, including soil, provide microbial taxa that are largely absent from highly sanitized urban environments and processed diets.

Soil hosts bacteria, fungi, protozoa and other microbes that contribute to ecosystem nutrient cycling. When people garden—through digging, turning compost, transplanting seedlings or simply handling soil—they can encounter microbes that transiently or persistently colonize the skin and, in some cases, the gut. These exposures appear to increase microbial diversity in some individuals, which is generally considered beneficial for community resilience.

## How soil microbes may reach the gut

Soil-to-gut transmission occurs through direct hand-to-mouth transfer, incidental ingestion of small amounts of soil on homegrown produce, or inhalation of particulate matter that subsequently settles and is swallowed. Observational household studies have linked routine gardening with detectable soil-derived taxa in fecal samples of participants, supporting the concept that environmental microbes can integrate into human microbial communities over time.

Research is ongoing to determine which soil taxa are most likely to persist in the gut, which are transient, and what functional roles they play. Current evidence favors a model in which repeated, low-level exposure promotes diversity and provides signals that modulate immune responses and metabolic processes rather than establishing permanent large-scale community shifts.

## Potential benefits and limitations

Increased microbial diversity is associated with improved metabolic flexibility and more robust immune regulation in a number of studies, though causality is difficult to establish from observational data alone. Gardening is also associated with reduced stress and improved mood, and the gut–brain axis provides a plausible biological pathway linking environmental microbial exposure to mental health outcomes.

However, soil is heterogeneous and can include opportunistic pathogens or contaminants depending on local conditions. Safe practices—washing produce, using well-managed compost, and basic hand hygiene after soil contact—help mitigate risks while maintaining beneficial exposure.

## Conclusion

Soil exposure through gardening is a plausible contributor to gut microbial diversity and may support aspects of immune and mental health. Future controlled studies will clarify the magnitude and mechanisms of benefit, but current evidence supports cautious, routine engagement with diverse outdoor environments as one component of a microbiome-aware lifestyle.
