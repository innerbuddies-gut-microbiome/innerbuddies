Leaky gut, clinically referred to as increased intestinal permeability, occurs when the tight junctions between intestinal epithelial cells become compromised. This can allow microbial products, food antigens, and toxins to cross into circulation, provoking inflammation and a range of systemic symptoms such as bloating, fatigue, skin issues, and food sensitivities. Understanding the role of the gut microbiome in barrier integrity is essential for evidence-based recovery.

Microbiome analysis can reveal microbial patterns linked to permeability: low abundances of mucin-supporting species (e.g., Akkermansia muciniphila), reduced butyrate producers (e.g., Faecalibacterium prausnitzii), or overgrowths of opportunistic organisms. A targeted test can therefore guide interventions rather than relying on a generic protocol. Dietary foundation

Restoring intestinal integrity often begins with diet. Emphasize whole foods that support microbial diversity and mucosal repair: a variety of fibers (vegetables, legumes, oats), polyphenol-rich fruits and teas, omega-3 fatty acids, and sources of gelatin or collagen such as bone broth for amino acids like glutamine. Include prebiotic foods (onion, garlic, leek, chicory) and, where tolerated, fermented foods (sauerkraut, kefir, miso) to encourage beneficial strains.

Concurrently, reduce exposures that commonly aggravate permeability: high refined-sugar intake, ultraprocessed foods with emulsifiers, excessive alcohol, and frequent NSAID use. If microbiome results indicate yeast or bacterial overgrowth, specific dietary adjustments (e.g., reduced simple sugars) may be advised.

Lifestyle and supportive therapies

Non-dietary factors strongly influence recovery. Prioritize sleep (7–9 hours), moderate regular exercise, and stress-reduction techniques (breathwork, mindfulness) because the brain–gut axis affects microbial balance and immune signaling. Adequate hydration and paced, mindful eating help maintain mucosal function and digestive efficiency.

Targeted supplementation and botanicals

Supplements can be used selectively and ideally guided by test results. Evidence-supported options include L-glutamine for enterocyte support, zinc (zinc carnosine where available) for tight-junction integrity, and butyrate or butyrate-promoting strategies to support colonocyte health. Strain-specific probiotics (e.g., Lactobacillus plantarum for junction support, Saccharomyces boulardii for yeast control) can be beneficial when matched to identified imbalances. Herbal antimicrobials such as berberine or oregano may be considered for overgrowths, but should be used under clinical supervision to avoid disrupting beneficial communities.

Monitoring and iterative care

Objective monitoring is valuable: repeating microbiome analysis every 3–6 months can document improvements in diversity, SCFA production, and reductions in pathogenic markers. Symptom tracking alongside test results helps refine diet, supplements, and lifestyle choices. Healing intestinal permeability is typically multifactorial: dietary optimization, lifestyle modification, targeted supplementation, and microbiome-guided adjustments.
