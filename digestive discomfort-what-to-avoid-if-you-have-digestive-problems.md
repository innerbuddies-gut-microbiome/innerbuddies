## What not to eat if you have digestive problems

Digestive problems—IBS, leaky gut, chronic bloating, and related disorders—are influenced strongly by diet and the gut microbiome. Certain foods commonly worsen symptoms by promoting inflammation, disrupting microbial balance, or irritating the intestinal lining. This concise guide outlines foods to avoid, why they cause issues, and evidence-based strategies to personalize choices.

### Common food categories to avoid

- Processed and ultra-processed foods: Packaged snacks, processed meats, and ready meals often contain emulsifiers, preservatives, and added sugars that can disrupt microbiota diversity and increase gut permeability.
- High-fat, fried foods: These slow gastric emptying and can aggravate reflux, bloating, and nausea in sensitive individuals.
- Refined sugars and some sweeteners: Excessive sugar feeds opportunistic microbes and yeast. Sugar alcohols (e.g., sorbitol, xylitol) and certain non-nutritive sweeteners can ferment in the colon and cause gas and diarrhea.
- Dairy and lactose-containing products: Lactose intolerance is common; without adequate lactase (or supporting microbes), dairy may cause cramping, gas, and loose stools.
- Gluten-containing grains: Beyond celiac disease, non-celiac gluten sensitivity may provoke symptoms in some people and contribute to low-grade inflammation.
- Spicy and highly acidic foods: These can irritate the gastric mucosa and exacerbate acid reflux or gastritis.
- Carbonated beverages and alcohol: Both increase luminal gas and can worsen bloating; alcohol also alters microbiome composition and gut barrier integrity.
- Certain high-FODMAP foods: Onions, garlic, legumes, and some fruits are fermentable carbohydrates that commonly trigger bloating in IBS.

### Why these foods cause problems

Many problematic foods either feed pathogenic microbes, contain compounds that directly inflame the gut lining, or are poorly digested and fermented by colonic bacteria. Chronic exposure to pro-inflammatory dietary patterns (high in trans fats, refined sugars, and industrial seed oils) is associated with dysbiosis and increased intestinal permeability in experimental and observational studies.

### Practical, evidence-based strategies

- Prioritize whole, minimally processed foods and dietary diversity to support microbial richness.
- Replace fried foods with baked or steamed options and choose anti-inflammatory fats (e.g., omega-3–rich sources) over high-omega-6 industrial oils.
- Limit refined sugar and problematic sweeteners; prefer natural, low-fermentable carbohydrate options when needed.
- Work with a clinician or dietitian to interpret testing and guide reintroduction safely.
