Wondering whether your gut microbiome could be contributing to persistent symptoms? A 'bad' gut microbiome—more accurately called dysbiosis—is characterized by reduced microbial diversity, overgrowth of opportunistic organisms, and altered metabolic function. This article summarizes common indicators, testing approaches, and how to interpret results in an evidence-based way.

### Common signs that suggest microbiome imbalance

Symptoms alone cannot diagnose dysbiosis, but several recurring patterns raise suspicion:

- Bloating, excess gas, and abdominal discomfort that are frequent or chronic.
- Irregular bowel habits, including persistent diarrhea, constipation, or alternating patterns.
- Unexplained fatigue or nutrient deficiencies (iron, B vitamins) despite normal intake.
- Skin issues such as acne, eczema, or rosacea that correlate with digestive complaints.
- Increased susceptibility to infections or prolonged recovery from minor illnesses.
- Mood changes, anxiety, or brain fog linked to gut disturbances via the gut–brain axis.

When several of these occur together, targeted testing can clarify whether the microbiome is a contributing factor.

### How testing evaluates your gut microbiome

Modern analyses use DNA-based methods to profile the microbes present and infer function. Common tests include 16S rRNA sequencing for taxonomic overview and metagenomic sequencing for species-level resolution and functional potential. Stool-based assays measure species richness, diversity, and relative abundances of key groups like Lactobacillus, Bifidobacteria, Firmicutes, and Bacteroidetes. They may also flag overrepresented pathogens (e.g., certain Clostridium strains or Candida) and report on markers associated with inflammation or gut permeability.

Laboratory results are most useful when paired with clinical context: diet, medications (especially antibiotics and proton-pump inhibitors), travel history, and symptoms. Stool tests sample the colonic microbiota and may not fully reflect the small intestine, where conditions like SIBO occur.

### Interpreting results and next steps

Key metrics to watch for in reports are diversity indices, presence/absence of beneficial taxa, and functional markers such as short-chain fatty acid production. Low diversity and decreased butyrate-producers tend to correlate with inflammation and reduced resilience. Conversely, overgrowths or imbalances may suggest targeted dietary changes, prebiotic support, or strain-specific probiotic interventions rather than broad-spectrum approaches.
