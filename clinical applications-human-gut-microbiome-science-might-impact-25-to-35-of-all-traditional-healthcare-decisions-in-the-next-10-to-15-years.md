The accelerating science of the human gut microbiome is beginning to intersect with multiple clinical domains. If microbiome-informed diagnostics and therapeutics become routine in clinical workflows, a conservative projection is that microbiome data could plausibly influence roughly 25–35% of traditional healthcare decisions within the next 10–15 years. This estimate is driven by the microbiome’s relevance across large-cost areas (metabolic, gastrointestinal, immunology) and its emerging role in high-cost specialty care (oncology, neurology).

Key domains and likely points of integration

Metabolic health (obesity, type 2 diabetes, NAFLD)

Today, metabolic disease represents one of the largest drivers of health system spending globally. Microbiome science may contribute to risk stratification, early detection, and personalized therapeutic strategies. Practical applications include microbiome-based prognostic signatures, diet-personalization guided by microbiome profiles, and targeted live biotherapeutics. Because metabolic conditions already account for a substantial share of healthcare expenditure, microbiome-informed approaches could influence care pathways for an estimated 20–25% of healthcare spending in many high-income settings.

Oncology

Cancer care is costly per case and a focus of intensive pharmaceutical investment. Evidence is accumulating that gut microbiome composition can predict response to checkpoint inhibitors and other immunotherapies, and that modulation of the microbiome may alter efficacy or adverse event profiles. While oncology represents a smaller fraction of case numbers, its high per-patient costs mean microbiome insights could meaningfully inform treatment decisions for a non-trivial share of oncology patients (estimates here are on the order of single-digit percentages to low double-digits within a decade, depending on adoption and validation).

Gastrointestinal disorders

Direct microbiome interventions—such as fecal microbiota transplant (FMT), targeted probiotics, and microbiome-derived small molecules—are most advanced in GI medicine. Diagnostic use (e.g., predicting IBD flares or stratifying IBS subtypes) and direct therapies are likely to become standard of care in many GI settings; it is plausible that microbiome data may guide decisions in a majority of GI consultations (estimates 50–70%). Neuropsychiatric and neurodegenerative conditions (gut–brain axis)

Mechanistic and clinical evidence is emerging for links between the gut microbiome and mood disorders, autism spectrum conditions, Parkinson’s disease, and other neuropsychiatric or neurodegenerative disorders. Early adoption is likely to be as adjunctive biomarkers and adjunct therapies rather than stand-alone cures; medium-term influence on neuro/psych care could be modest (a few percent of decisions) but may grow with stronger causal evidence.

Immunology and inflammatory disease

Microbiome signatures can reflect immune system priming and may predict susceptibility to autoimmune or inflammatory flares (RA, MS, psoriasis, severe allergies). Integration of microbiome information into biologic selection or dosing strategies could affect treatment planning for a meaningful share of autoimmune care (estimates 10–20%).

Context, uncertainty, and resources

Uncertainty remains: effect sizes, reproducibility across populations, regulatory pathways, and cost-effectiveness will shape adoption.
