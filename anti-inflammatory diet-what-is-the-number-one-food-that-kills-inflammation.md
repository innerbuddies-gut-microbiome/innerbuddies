Inflammation is a protective process, but when it becomes chronic it contributes to conditions such as cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and autoimmune disorders. Diet shapes systemic inflammation largely through its effects on the gut microbiome, and targeted food choices can shift microbial communities toward a less inflammatory state.

## The evidence-based top food

Among whole foods, fatty fish—particularly wild-caught salmon—stands out as the most consistently supported anti-inflammatory choice. Rich in long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA), salmon helps reduce inflammatory eicosanoids and cytokines and supports the formation of specialized pro-resolving mediators that actively resolve inflammation. Clinical studies associate regular omega-3 intake with lower markers like C-reactive protein and improved outcomes in inflammatory conditions.

## How the gut microbiome mediates effects

Dietary omega-3s also interact with gut bacteria: they can increase microbial diversity, favor beneficial taxa, and reduce translocation of endotoxins that drive systemic inflammation. Conversely, diets high in processed fats and refined sugars can promote dysbiosis and intestinal permeability. Microbiome testing can reveal individual imbalances—such as low butyrate-producing species or overgrowths of pro-inflammatory strains—that influence how a person responds to dietary changes.

## Foods and strategies that complement fatty fish

- Polyphenol-rich fruits (blueberries, pomegranates) and green tea: act as antioxidants and prebiotics.  
- Fermented foods and targeted probiotics: support barrier function and competitive exclusion of pathogens.  
- High-fiber vegetables and legumes: feed butyrate-producing bacteria that strengthen the gut lining.  
- Anti-inflammatory spices (turmeric, ginger): modulate inflammatory signaling pathways and can be used with fats to improve absorption.

When personalization is needed—such as histamine sensitivity or methane-dominant dysbiosis—tailored protocols informed by testing provide safer, more effective adjustments. ## Practical guidance

Aim to include fatty fish such as wild-caught salmon 2–3 times weekly while also prioritizing a variety of plant fibers, fermented foods, and polyphenol-rich produce. For people interested in individualized insights, pairing dietary adjustments with microbiome data can clarify which foods to emphasize or avoid. ## Summary

Wild-caught fatty fish, due to its EPA and DHA content, is the single food most consistently associated with inflammation reduction. Its benefits are amplified when combined with a diverse, fiber-rich diet that supports beneficial gut microbes and reduces intestinal permeability.
