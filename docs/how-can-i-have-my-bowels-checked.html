A bowel check evaluates intestinal structure and function to detect inflammation, infection, or other gastrointestinal issues. Advances in gut microbiome science mean that modern bowel checks often combine traditional diagnostics (imaging, endoscopy, blood tests) with stool-based microbiome testing to provide a more functional picture of digestive health.

What microbiome testing adds to a bowel check

Microbiome testing analyzes the microbial communities in stool—bacteria, viruses, fungi and other organisms—and assesses diversity and the presence of potentially harmful species. Unlike imaging or colonoscopy, which identify structural abnormalities, sequencing-based tests and targeted qPCR panels reveal ecosystem-level information that can explain symptoms like bloating, irregular stools, or fatigue. Common stool-based testing methods

Major microbiome and stool analysis methods include:

  • 16S rRNA gene sequencing — identifies bacterial groups and estimates diversity.
  • Whole genome shotgun sequencing — higher resolution for bacteria, fungi and viruses; can identify species or strains.
  • qPCR panels — targeted and quantitative for known pathogens or functional genes.

Each method has strengths and limits: 16S is cost-effective for diversity metrics, shotgun sequencing is more comprehensive, and qPCR gives quantitative data for specific microbes. Choosing a test should depend on clinical questions and available interpretation support.

Preparing for accurate stool testing

To ensure samples reflect your usual gut state, avoid antibiotics and probiotics for 2–3 weeks before sampling unless advised otherwise, and refrain from drastic dietary changes in the days prior. If you are scheduled for a colonoscopy, collect any stool sample before starting bowel prep because cleansing agents alter microbial communities. Practical instructions for safe collection and handling are usually provided with kit-based services.

How to interpret results and integrate with other tests

Microbiome reports typically include diversity metrics, relative abundances, and possible pathogenic markers; some also provide inferred metabolite or short-chain fatty acid profiles. These findings are best interpreted alongside clinical history, blood tests, imaging, breath tests (for SIBO), or endoscopy. Some at-home kits also provide DNA-based stool testing options that can be useful when combined with clinical evaluation. Test frequency depends on clinical needs: during active treatment or monitoring, re-testing every 3–6 months may be appropriate; otherwise annual checks can track long-term trends.

Summary

Having your bowels checked can range from conventional stool tests and colonoscopy to functional microbiome assessments. Combining structural and microbial data produces a fuller view of gut health and supports targeted interventions. Discuss testing options and result interpretation with a healthcare professional to ensure findings are integrated into a safe, evidence-based care plan.
