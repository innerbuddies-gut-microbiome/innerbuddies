Healthy intestines support digestion, immune function, and metabolic health. Modern gut microbiome testing can reveal the composition and function of the microbial community in your stool, making it possible to tailor interventions that address root causes rather than symptoms. This article summarizes evidence-based strategies to support intestinal wellness, guided by personalized data and general best practices.

Assessing intestinal health with microbiome data

Microbiome analysis identifies diversity, the presence of beneficial taxa (for example, Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus), and markers associated with inflammation or dysbiosis. Knowing these features helps prioritize interventions: increasing fiber diversity when diversity is low, addressing overgrowth when pathogens are detected, or supporting short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) producers if SCFA output is reduced. For an overview of practical testing options, see microbiome testing.

Dietary patterns that support intestinal function

A diverse, plant-forward diet provides a range of fermentable fibers and polyphenols that feed beneficial microbes and promote SCFA production, which nourishes colonocytes and modulates inflammation. Fermented foods such as yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, and tempeh can introduce live microbes, while prebiotic-rich foods—onions, garlic, asparagus, oats, and resistant starch—help sustain them. If testing reveals specific deficits, dietary choices can be refined to encourage growth of keystone species.

Targeted interventions informed by testing

Test results often suggest targeted measures: strain-specific probiotics to replenish low taxa, prebiotics to boost SCFA producers, or short-term dietary adjustments (e.g., low-FODMAP for symptom relief under supervision) when overgrowths are present. Repeating tests periodically enables tracking of microbial shifts and assessment of intervention efficacy.

Lifestyle factors that influence the gut

Stress, sleep, physical activity, and medication use substantially shape microbial communities. Chronic stress can increase intestinal permeability and reduce microbial richness, while regular moderate exercise correlates with greater diversity. Prioritize consistent sleep, stress-reduction practices (mindfulness, breathwork), and movement routines to support a resilient microbiome. Limit unnecessary antibiotic exposure and review chronic medication impacts with a clinician when possible.

Practical tips and continued monitoring

Adopt incremental changes—introduce new plant foods gradually, incorporate fermented foods several times per week, and use supplements or antimicrobials only when guided by evidence or a practitioner.
