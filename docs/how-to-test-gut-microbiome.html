How can I test my gut microbiome?

The gut microbiome plays a central role in digestion, immunity, and even mental health. Testing your gut microbiome can reveal which microorganisms are present, how diverse your community is, and which functions those microbes might be performing. This article summarizes common testing approaches, what results typically mean, and practical next steps for interpreting findings.

Testing methods: what to expect

Most commercial and clinical gut microbiome tests analyze a stool sample. Common laboratory approaches include 16S rRNA sequencing, which identifies bacterial groups at the genus level, and metagenomic sequencing, which provides species-level resolution and detects bacteria, fungi, viruses, and microbial genes. More advanced approaches, such as metatranscriptomics, assess active gene expression but are less commonly used for general consumer testing.

When choosing a test consider the depth of information you need: 16S-based reports are often sufficient to assess overall diversity and major shifts, while metagenomics can identify specific strains and functional capabilities like short-chain fatty acid production or inflammatory gene signatures. What a comprehensive gut health assessment includes

A thorough gut health assessment combines microbial analysis with clinical markers and symptom history. Beyond taxonomy, useful markers include measures of inflammation (e.g., calprotectin), metabolites such as short-chain fatty acids, digestive enzyme activity, and indicators of gut permeability. Integrating symptom patterns—like bloating, irregular bowel movements, or brain fog—helps contextualize lab findings and prioritize interventions.

Interpreting results and limitations

Typical report sections describe diversity, relative abundance of key groups, and any detected pathogens or overgrowths. Lower diversity is often correlated with reduced resilience, but interpretation should be individualized: some people do well with lower diversity depending on diet and environment. Keep in mind that stool testing reflects luminal communities and may not fully represent mucosal-associated microbes or small intestinal populations (e.g., SIBO requires different diagnostics).

Practical considerations

Pre-test conditions can influence results: recent antibiotics, probiotics, major dietary changes, or travel may alter microbial composition. For more about how travel can affect results and how labs account for it, see this discussion on traveling and gut microbiome test results and the related overview at Can Traveling Affect Your Gut Microbiome Test Results?. After testing, evidence-based next steps often include targeted dietary adjustments (increasing diverse fibers, fermented foods when appropriate), addressing identified pathogens or imbalances under clinical supervision, and periodic retesting to track changes. For details on kit logistics and sample collection, see product information such as the Microbiome Test page.

In summary, microbiome testing can provide valuable, individualized insight when interpreted alongside symptoms and clinical markers. Choose a testing approach that matches your goals, be mindful of pre-test factors that influence results, and use findings as one component of a broader, evidence-based plan for gut health.
