Microbiota are the complex communities of microorganisms that live on and inside the human body. They include bacteria, fungi, viruses, archaea, and sometimes protozoa. Types of microbiota are commonly classified by location — gut, skin, oral, nasal and urogenital — and by function, such as commensals, probiotics, pathobionts and opportunists. Understanding these categories helps clarify how microbial ecosystems influence digestion, immunity and overall health.

Where microbiota live and why it matters

Different body sites support distinct microbial communities shaped by pH, oxygen availability, moisture and host defenses. The gut microbiota, concentrated in the colon, is the densest and most metabolically active, producing short‑chain fatty acids, synthesizing vitamins, and participating in immune education. Skin microbiota vary with region (oily, moist or dry) and protect against pathogens, while oral and nasal communities influence respiratory and dental health. Vaginal microbiota play key roles in reproductive tract protection and pathogen exclusion.

Functional categories of microbiota

Microbes are often described by their roles: probiotics are live microbes with demonstrated beneficial effects; commensals coexist without obvious benefit or harm under normal conditions; pathobionts are members of the normal microbiota that can contribute to disease under altered conditions; and opportunistic pathogens cause disease when host resistance is compromised. These functional labels are context dependent and can change with shifts in diet, medication or immune status.

Gut microbiota: dominant groups and diversity

Four bacterial phyla frequently dominate gut surveys: Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria and Proteobacteria. Within these phyla are genera implicated in fiber fermentation, SCFA production and mucosal health, such as Faecalibacterium, Bacteroides and Bifidobacterium. Microbiome diversity — measured as richness and evenness using sequencing methods — is an important marker: higher diversity is often associated with metabolic resilience and lower risk of some chronic diseases, while reduced diversity is linked to conditions like inflammatory bowel disease and obesity.

Modern sequencing approaches, including 16S rRNA gene profiling and shotgun metagenomics, enable population‑level and individual assessments. Microbial interactions and systemic effects

Microbiota form interactive networks that sustain community functions through cooperation and competition. Gut microbes produce metabolites that modulate immunity and can affect distant organs via the gut‑brain and gut‑skin axes.
