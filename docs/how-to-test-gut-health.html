How can I test my gut health?

Assessing gut health combines symptom tracking, targeted laboratory tests, and interpretation of biological markers to form a clear picture of digestive and intestinal function. This article summarizes the most useful, evidence-based options for evaluating microbiome composition, digestive performance, and intestinal barrier integrity so you can discuss practical next steps with a healthcare provider.

Symptom appraisal and preliminary screening

Begin with a concise symptom history: patterns of bloating, irregular bowel movements, persistent diarrhea, heartburn, unexplained fatigue, or food reactions. A short food-and-symptom diary over two to four weeks often reveals correlations that guide testing choices. Basic blood work (CBC, metabolic panel, thyroid function, and nutrient levels) can screen for systemic causes that affect gut function.

Digestive function tests

Stool and breath tests are primary tools for evaluating how the gut digests and moves food. Stool analysis can reveal markers of inflammation, fat malabsorption, pathogen overgrowth, and pancreatic enzyme insufficiency. Breath tests (hydrogen/methane) are used clinically to detect bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine (SIBO). Additional options include pancreatic elastase to assess exocrine pancreatic function and transit-time studies when constipation or motility issues are prominent.

Intestinal barrier and immune markers

Intestinal permeability and mucosal immunity testing help identify barrier dysfunction and inflammatory activity. Common assessments include lactulose/mannitol permeability tests, zonulin measurements (a regulator of tight junctions), and fecal secretory IgA, calprotectin, or lactoferrin to quantify mucosal inflammation. Elevated inflammatory markers may warrant referral for further gastroenterology evaluation for conditions such as IBD.

Microbiome analysis

Microbiome testing uses stool samples to characterize bacterial diversity and functional potential. Methods range from 16S rRNA sequencing, which profiles bacterial taxa, to whole-metagenome sequencing, which provides higher resolution and functional insights. Results commonly report diversity indices, relative abundance of beneficial versus opportunistic taxa, and inferred metabolic functions such as short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production. These data can inform dietary fiber strategies, targeted prebiotic choices, and rational probiotic selection.
