Can you have your microbiome tested?

Your gut microbiome is a complex ecosystem that influences digestion, immunity, metabolism, and even mood. Microbiome testing offers a window into this system by analysing the microorganisms in your digestive tract. This article explains what these tests measure, common methodologies, and how results can be interpreted responsibly.

What a microbiome test measures

Most gut tests start with a stool sample and report on microbial composition (which species are present), relative abundance (how common each species is), and diversity (how many different species exist and how evenly they are distributed). Advanced reports may also include functional insights, such as genes related to short-chain fatty acid production or markers of inflammation and gut barrier integrity.

How testing works

Laboratories typically use 16S rRNA gene sequencing to identify bacterial genera or metagenomic sequencing to capture broader genetic content and strain-level resolution. Labs may complement sequencing with biochemical assays that measure inflammatory markers, digestive enzyme levels, or detect pathogens. Interpreting results

Key metrics to watch include diversity indices (e.g., Shannon index), the presence or absence of beneficial taxa (such as butyrate producers), and any flagged opportunistic organisms. Low diversity can indicate vulnerability to dysbiosis, while specific overgrowths may suggest targeted interventions. Biochemical markers help contextualize sequencing data; for instance, elevated calprotectin implies inflammation that sequencing alone won’t explain.

What you can and cannot learn

Microbiome tests can provide useful information about microbial patterns tied to symptoms like bloating, irregular bowel habits, or recurring infections. However, they are not diagnostic on their own for complex chronic diseases and should be interpreted alongside clinical history and, when appropriate, medical testing. Longitudinal testing—repeating assessments over months—can show the effect of dietary or lifestyle changes on microbial communities.

Using results to guide choices

Actionable strategies often focus on dietary diversity (more varied plant fibers), measured probiotic or prebiotic interventions, and addressing inflammation or malabsorption when indicated.
