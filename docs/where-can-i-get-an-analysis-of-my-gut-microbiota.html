Where can I get an analysis of my gut microbiota?

Gut microbiota analysis provides a snapshot of the trillions of microbes in your digestive tract and can help explain effects on digestion, immunity, metabolism and mood. Tests range from basic community profiling to multi-omic screens that include functional genes and metabolites. Choosing the right option depends on whether your goal is clinical diagnosis, symptom troubleshooting, or general wellness optimization.

Where to find testing services

Options include clinical laboratories (ordered by a healthcare provider), specialty gastroenterology clinics, research programs, and direct-to-consumer kits for at-home stool collection. Clinical labs are appropriate when diagnostic clarity is needed and often combine pathogen detection and inflammation markers with microbiome profiling. Consumer kits are accessible for baseline monitoring and lifestyle-guided changes; for a practical overview, consult this guide on where to get an analysis of my gut microbiota.

What different tests measure

Two commonly used sequencing approaches are 16S rRNA gene sequencing, which profiles bacterial genera and community diversity, and shotgun metagenomics, which identifies species-level taxa and functional genes (for example, butyrate synthesis or antibiotic-resistance markers). More comprehensive services may add metabolomics (measuring short-chain fatty acids and bile acids) and host biomarkers such as fecal calprotectin. Understand the method, the lab's quality standards, and whether results are clinician-reviewed before selecting a service.

Practical considerations for stool sampling

Stool is the standard sample for gut microbiota assessment because it reflects the distal gut community. Home kits usually include a sterile collection device, stabilization buffer, and shipping materials. Follow provider instructions on pausing antibiotics or probiotics, maintain typical diet for several days before sampling, and ship samples within the recommended timeframe. Some labs request multiple samples across days to reduce variability.

Interpreting and acting on results

Reports typically show diversity scores, relative abundances, and functional annotations. Look for consistent patterns—low diversity, reduced fiber-fermenting taxa, or elevated Proteobacteria—rather than single outlying numbers. Evidence-based actions often include increasing dietary fiber diversity, targeted probiotic strains supported by clinical data for specific symptoms, and lifestyle changes that support microbial resilience. Clinically concerning findings (pathogens, elevated inflammatory markers) should prompt discussion with a healthcare professional.

Choosing a reputable provider

Key selection criteria are transparent methodology, lab accreditation, clear interpretation, data-privacy policies, and follow-up support.
