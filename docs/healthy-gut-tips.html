What to do to have a healthy gut?

A healthy gut supports digestion, immunity, mood regulation, and energy. The gut microbiome—a complex community of bacteria, viruses and fungi—helps break down fiber, synthesize vitamins, and regulate inflammation. Evidence-based strategies that combine testing, diet, and lifestyle can improve microbial diversity and digestive function.

Use microbiome insights to personalize care

Microbiome testing reveals the composition of your gut flora and highlights specific deficits or overgrowths that may underlie symptoms. For tailored interpretation, consider a validated gut microbiome assessment such as a microbiome test. Results can guide dietary changes, targeted probiotic strains, and prebiotic choices rather than relying on one-size-fits-all approaches.

Dietary patterns that support diversity

Diet is the primary driver of microbiome composition. A plant-forward approach that emphasizes a variety of fibers—vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts and seeds—feeds beneficial bacteria and increases short-chain fatty acid production. Fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso) provide live microbes and fermentation metabolites that support gut barrier function.

Polyphenol-rich foods such as berries, green tea and olives modulate microbial activity and favor health-associated species. Limiting added sugars, highly processed foods, artificial sweeteners and excessive industrial seed oils reduces selective pressures that can promote pathogenic strains.

Probiotics, prebiotics, and synbiotics

Probiotics (specific strains of bacteria or yeast) can be useful for discrete indications—post-antibiotic recovery, acute infectious diarrhea, or some functional bowel disorders—when evidence supports their use. Strain selection matters: benefits are strain-specific and dose-dependent. Prebiotics (non-digestible fibers like inulin, FOS, GOS) nourish resident beneficial microbes; combining probiotics with appropriate prebiotics (synbiotics) may improve colonization and outcomes. Introduce concentrated prebiotic supplements gradually to reduce gas and bloating.

Lifestyle factors that markedly influence the gut

Non-dietary behaviors shape microbial ecology. Regular moderate exercise is associated with higher diversity and increased production of anti-inflammatory metabolites. Adequate sleep and consistent circadian timing support digestive rhythms and microbial activity. Chronic psychological stress alters gut motility, immunity, and microbial balance; mind-body practices such as meditation, breathing exercises, or consistent social support can mitigate these effects.

Antibiotics are sometimes necessary but also disruptive; when used, strategies to support recovery include diet adjustments, selected probiotics, and follow-up testing to assess resilience.

Conclusion
