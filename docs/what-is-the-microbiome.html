The microbiome refers to the collection of microorganisms and their genetic material that live on and within the human body. Most attention focuses on the gut microbiome housed in the large intestine, where bacteria, viruses, fungi, archaea, and protozoa form a complex ecological community that influences digestion, immunity, and systemic physiology.

Structure and function of the gut microbiome

Gut microbes participate in the breakdown of dietary fibers, production of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) such as butyrate, and synthesis of vitamins like K and certain B vitamins. Dominant bacterial phyla—Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria, and Proteobacteria—have complementary roles: some specialize in fiber degradation, others in maintaining mucosal integrity or modulating immune signaling. Viruses, mainly bacteriophages, and low-abundance fungi and archaea contribute to ecological balance and metabolic outcomes.

Health implications and balance

Microbial balance is important for resilient digestion and immune regulation. Dysbiosis, a shift in community composition or function, has been associated with conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease, metabolic disorders, allergies, and emerging links to mood and cognition via the gut–brain axis. Diversity—both richness and evenness of species—is a commonly used indicator of ecosystem robustness; lower diversity often correlates with greater disease risk in observational studies.

Assessment and practical insights

Advances in sequencing enable noninvasive assessment of the gut microbiome through stool-based testing. Modern approaches include 16S rRNA gene sequencing for bacterial community profiling and whole-genome shotgun metagenomics for species-level identification and functional potential. These tests can quantify diversity metrics, identify overrepresented or underrepresented taxa, and suggest potential functional deficits such as reduced SCFA production.
