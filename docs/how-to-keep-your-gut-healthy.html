What to Do to Keep Your Gut Healthy?

Maintaining a healthy gut supports digestion, immune function, metabolism, and even mood. The gut microbiome—a complex community of bacteria, fungi, viruses, and other microbes—shapes many aspects of physiology. Practical, evidence-based strategies can preserve microbial diversity, support the intestinal barrier, and reduce inflammation. For an individualized plan, consult the data in this comprehensive guide to gut health.

Understand the microbiome and interpret data

Microbiome testing identifies which species are present, their relative abundance, and diversity metrics. Results can reveal dysbiosis patterns—loss of beneficial strains, overgrowth of opportunists, or low diversity—that guide targeted interventions. If you use a validated home test such as the InnerBuddies microbiome test, focus on actionable findings like reduced butyrate-producers or low Bifidobacteria and prioritize appropriate diet and lifestyle changes.

Dietary foundations for microbial diversity

Dietary variety is the strongest, most reproducible influence on microbial diversity. Aim to “eat the rainbow” of plant foods: fruits, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and seeds provide a range of prebiotic fibers that selectively feed beneficial bacteria. Fermented foods (e.g., yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut) can introduce live microbes and supportive metabolites; introduce them gradually to monitor tolerance.

Targeted nutritional and lifestyle strategies

Not every fiber or fermented product helps every person. Microbiome-informed choices can specify which fibers to emphasize or limit. For example, those sensitive to fermentable carbohydrates may benefit from lower-FODMAP options while rebuilding tolerance slowly. Hydration, regular physical activity, and consistent meal timing support transit and microbial rhythms. Prioritize 7–9 hours of sleep and structured stress-reduction practices—sleep disruption and chronic stress measurably alter microbiome composition.

Support the intestinal barrier and motility

Nutrients such as omega-3 fatty acids, glutamine, and collagen-rich broths can support mucosal repair; resistant starch and ghee are dietary sources linked to butyrate production. If testing indicates slow transit or methane-associated overgrowth, simple approaches such as optimized magnesium intake, hydration, and movement can improve motility. Consider repeating microbiome analysis periodically to confirm improvements; guidance on retesting intervals is available in this resource on test frequency.

Use monitoring tools and emerging technologies

Digital and in-home technologies are expanding ways to track bowel habits and biomarkers. For context on new monitoring approaches, see explanations of smart toilets and their applications as well as a broader discussion of the future of smart toilets and health monitoring. These tools may complement stool-based microbiome tests by providing longitudinal physiological data.

Practical precautions

Avoid unnecessary antibiotics and minimize ultra-processed foods, excess alcohol, and artificial sweeteners where possible. If supplements are used, select strains and prebiotics informed by your microbiome report rather than one-size-fits-all products.

Keeping your gut healthy is an incremental process that combines diverse, fiber-rich eating, adequate sleep and movement, measured use of supplements when indicated, and periodic microbiome-informed reassessment. Evidence-based, personalized choices tend to produce the most consistent improvements in gut function and resilience.
