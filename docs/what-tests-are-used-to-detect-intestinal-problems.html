Accurate diagnosis of intestinal problems relies on a combination of laboratory tests, functional assays, and imaging studies. Common presenting symptoms—bloating, abdominal pain, irregular bowel movements, unexplained fatigue—can reflect diverse underlying causes such as infection, inflammation, structural abnormalities, or microbial imbalance. This article summarizes the primary tests clinicians use to evaluate intestinal health and how each contributes to a differential diagnosis.

Stool Microbiome and Routine Stool Testing

Stool testing provides foundational information. Routine stool analysis can detect pathogens, parasites, blood, and signs of malabsorption, while stool microbiome sequencing (16S rRNA or shotgun metagenomics) characterizes community diversity and the presence of opportunistic organisms. Microbiome profiles help clinicians and researchers identify dysbiosis patterns associated with conditions like irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or inflammatory disorders. A laboratory or at-home kit such as the InnerBuddies microbiome test typically supplies a report on diversity, relative abundances, and flagged pathogens or resistance genes.

Breath Tests for SIBO and Carbohydrate Malabsorption

Breath tests measure exhaled hydrogen and methane after ingestion of a substrate (lactulose or glucose) to detect Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) and specific carbohydrate malabsorption. Hydrogen rises if bacteria ferment carbohydrates prematurely; methane is often associated with constipation-predominant presentations. These non-invasive tests complement stool-based analyses by focusing on the small intestine, where standard stool tests are less informative.

Fecal and Blood Inflammatory Markers

Fecal calprotectin and lactoferrin are sensitive markers of intestinal inflammation and are useful to differentiate inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) from functional disorders like IBS. Blood tests—complete blood count, C-reactive protein (CRP), and specific serologies such as tissue transglutaminase antibodies for celiac disease—help assess systemic inflammation, anemia, and autoimmune processes that affect gut function.

Food Sensitivity and Immune Response Testing

Allergy testing (skin prick or specific IgE) and broader immune profiling (IgG/IgA panels) can identify food-triggered immune responses. While sensitivity testing has limitations and should be interpreted in context, these results sometimes clarify dietary factors that perpetuate inflammation or alter microbial composition.

Endoscopy, Colonoscopy, and Imaging

Endoscopic procedures provide direct visualization and allow biopsies to confirm mucosal disease, such as Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis, celiac disease, or neoplasia. Cross-sectional imaging (CT, MRI) and ultrasound assess structural abnormalities, masses, strictures, and complications that may not be evident through laboratory testing alone.

Integrating Test Results for Personalized Care

No single test answers every question; clinicians commonly combine stool microbiome data, breath testing, inflammatory markers, and imaging to form a comprehensive view.
