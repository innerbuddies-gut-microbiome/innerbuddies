What is the best test for the microbiome?

Interest in the gut microbiome has led to many testing options aimed at mapping the microbes living in stool and assessing digestive function. Choosing the most appropriate test depends on the question you want answered: taxonomic composition, functional potential, or clinical markers of gut health. This article summarizes current methodologies and practical considerations for evidence-based decision making.

Testing methods and what they reveal

Two sequencing methods dominate microbiome analysis. 16S rRNA gene sequencing profiles bacterial genera and is cost-effective for broad community surveys. Shotgun metagenomic sequencing sequences all DNA in a sample, enabling species- and strain-level identification and detection of viruses, fungi, and microbial genes linked to metabolic pathways. For functional insight, metagenomics is generally more informative, while 16S remains useful for population-level comparisons.

Complementary approaches include metabolomics, which measures small molecules (e.g., short-chain fatty acids) produced by microbes, and transcriptomics, which captures gene expression. Combining these 'omics increases interpretive power but raises cost and analytic complexity.

Gut health versus microbiota profiling

Microbiota profiling answers “who” is present and in what relative abundance. Gut health screening addresses “how” the digestive system performs—measuring inflammation markers, enzyme activity, permeability indicators, and malabsorption. Integrating both data types yields a fuller clinical picture: for example, low levels of fiber-fermenting taxa alongside low SCFA concentrations and elevated inflammatory markers suggests interventions targeting fiber intake and mucosal healing.

Accuracy, interpretation, and clinical context

Shotgun metagenomics offers the highest resolution for microbial composition and functional gene detection, making it the most accurate single method for comprehensive profiling. However, lab methods, bioinformatics pipelines, and reference databases vary; accuracy also depends on proper sample collection, storage, and sequencing depth. Interpretation requires context—age, diet, medications (especially antibiotics), and clinical history all shape microbiome results.

Practical considerations

When selecting a test, evaluate sequencing depth, whether functional analysis or metabolite data are included, and how results are presented. Look for transparent methodology and access to raw data or clinician support for interpretation. For longitudinal monitoring, consistent sampling protocols improve comparability over time.
