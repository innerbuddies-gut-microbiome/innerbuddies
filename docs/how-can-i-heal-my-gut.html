How can I heal my gut?

Restoring gut health improves digestion, immune function, and cognitive clarity. Evidence-based gut healing focuses on identifying specific imbalances in the microbiome, repairing the intestinal barrier, and adopting sustainable dietary and lifestyle practices. Microbiome testing can provide individualized data that guides targeted interventions rather than one-size-fits-all approaches.

Why test the microbiome?

Microbiome analysis examines the species and functional capacity of microbes in a stool sample, revealing diversity, overgrowths, and metabolic markers. These findings can clarify causes of symptoms such as bloating, irregular stools, fatigue, or skin reactions. A structured report helps prioritize actions—whether increasing fiber to support butyrate producers, addressing fungal overgrowth, or adjusting dietary fats to manage bile-tolerant species. Core steps to promote intestinal repair

  • Resolve dysbiosis. Use targeted antimicrobials or probiotics based on test results; generic regimens may miss key culprits.
  • Support the mucosal barrier. Nutrients such as L-glutamine, zinc, and collagen, plus polyphenol-rich foods, help restore mucosal integrity and reduce permeability.
  • Repopulate beneficial strains. Prebiotic fibers and selected probiotic strains can rebuild depleted groups once pathogen burden is reduced.
  • Reduce ongoing insults. Minimize excessive sugars, processed fats, and chronic stressors that perpetuate inflammation.

Food and lifestyle considerations

Dietary diversity fosters microbial richness: rotate vegetables, include soluble fibers (oats, onions, leeks), and incorporate polyphenol sources like berries and green tea. Fermented foods can contribute live microbes if tolerated. Adequate hydration, sleep, regular physical activity, and stress reduction practices (breathwork, mindfulness) all support the gut–brain axis and recovery of digestive function.

Using test results to individualize care

Interpretation matters: low abundance of butyrate producers suggests increasing resistant starch and inulin-type prebiotics; elevated methane producers may require protocols to improve transit time; evidence of biofilms or opportunists can call for specific disruptors. Monitoring progress

Repeat testing at intervals (for many people, 3–6 months during active protocols, then annually) helps verify shifts in diversity and function and informs adjustments. Combining symptom tracking with sequential microbiome data reduces guesswork and supports durable repair.

When applied thoughtfully, microbiome-informed strategies align diet, supplements, and lifestyle to the specific needs of an individual, making gut healing a measurable and sustainable process.
