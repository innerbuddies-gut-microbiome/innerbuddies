Selecting the most effective probiotic depends less on brand and more on individual biology. Personalized gut microbiome testing reveals the composition and functional status of your intestinal ecosystem, guiding targeted probiotic choices that match specific deficiencies or imbalances.

Microbiome assays analyze a stool sample using genomic sequencing to identify organisms at the genus and strain levels. This information can show reduced diversity, low abundance of butyrate producers, or overgrowths of inflammatory taxa. Interpreting those findings enables a precision approach to supplementation and dietary adjustments rather than generalized recommendations.

For a high-level overview of how testing informs interventions, see this microbiome testing guide, which explains sample interpretation and common patterns clinicians encounter.

Specific strains have documented roles: for example, Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG can reduce certain types of diarrhea, Bifidobacterium longum supports fiber fermentation and short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, and Saccharomyces boulardii is useful for antibiotic-associated dysbiosis. A targeted report will indicate which of these or other strains are most relevant to your profile.

Testing also connects gut health to other domains. If your report suggests microbial patterns associated with mood or cognition, consult resources that explore this link further; for context on mental health correlations, consider this discussion on microbiome test and mental health and the related overview on mental health and mood.

When a test identifies low abundance of keystone or butyrate-producing taxa (such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii or Roseburia), interventions may combine specific probiotics with prebiotics that feed beneficial microbes. Prebiotic fibers like inulin or fructooligosaccharides (FOS) can support colonization and SCFA synthesis, improving barrier function and reducing inflammation.
