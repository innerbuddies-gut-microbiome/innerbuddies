Families share routines, spaces, and often the microbes that live within us. Research increasingly shows that cohabitation — eating the same meals, sharing surfaces, sleeping in the same home, and pet interactions — can make gut microbial communities more similar across household members. This article summarizes the mechanisms and implications of household microbiome similarity, drawing on population projects and family studies. What is the gut microbiome? The gut microbiome comprises trillions of bacteria, viruses, fungi, and archaea that inhabit the digestive tract. These microbes aid digestion, synthesize vitamins, shape immune development, communicate with the nervous system, and help protect against pathogens. The composition of this community is influenced by both host biology and environmental exposures. How do household members become similar? Multiple cohort studies report that cohabitating individuals frequently have more similar gut microbiota than unrelated people living apart. Shared features that promote similarity include common diets and mealtimes, the same water and air in a dwelling, shared household surfaces, and frequent physical contact. Pets — particularly dogs — also act as microbial bridges, increasing diversity in homes and transferring pet-associated microbes onto floors, furniture, and skin. Genes versus environment Twin studies and large cohorts indicate that while host genetics shape aspects of the gut environment (for example, mucin production, gut epithelial features, and immune signaling), environmental factors often dominate. Diet composition, antibiotic exposures, hygiene habits, and daily routines can override genetic predispositions to favor similar microbial profiles among people who live together. Notably, some projects have observed that shared living space predicts microbial resemblance as strongly as, or more than, genetic relatedness in certain contexts. Life stages and household dynamics Microbial sharing begins early: infants acquire initial microbes from mothers during birth and breastfeeding, and siblings and caregivers further diversify that early community through play, shared toys, and infection–recovery cycles. Over decades, families may age together and undergo parallel microbiome shifts — for instance, a decline in diversity in older adults or a shift toward species associated with higher protein intake. Shared medications (e.g., proton pump inhibitors or statins) can also alter gut flora across household members. Health implications Household microbial similarity has mixed consequences. On one hand, beneficial strains can propagate resilience against pathogens and support metabolic health; on the other, shared exposures may increase susceptibility to familial patterns of metabolic or inflammatory conditions. This has led researchers to explore family-based interventions — dietary modifications or targeted probiotics — aimed at improving gut ecology at the household level.
