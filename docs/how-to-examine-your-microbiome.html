Examining your microbiome can clarify causes of digestive symptoms, immune changes, and metabolic shifts. Modern stool-based analyses use DNA sequencing to profile bacteria, fungi, and other microbes; understanding what these tests measure helps you interpret results and make evidence-based adjustments.

Which tests are commonly used?

Two main laboratory approaches are 16S rRNA gene sequencing and whole metagenomic sequencing. 16S sequencing identifies bacterial taxa at the genus level and is cost-effective for broad community profiling. Metagenomic sequencing reads whole genomes from the sample, providing higher taxonomic resolution and the ability to detect nonbacterial organisms and functional genes. Some tests also include targeted qPCR panels for specific pathogens or markers of inflammation.

Preparing and collecting a sample

Accuracy begins with consistent sampling. Avoid antibiotics for at least four weeks before testing and maintain your typical diet for several days prior to collection to capture a representative microbial state. Most home kits use a small stool sample collected with a supplied tool and returned by mail. Follow kit instructions precisely to reduce contamination and ensure reliable laboratory processing; for procedural guidance see the How to examine your microbiome resource.

What results typically show

Reports commonly include diversity metrics (alpha and beta diversity), relative abundances of dominant taxa, and flags for low or elevated levels of specific organisms. Some analyses estimate functional potential—such as short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production—or note the presence of species linked to inflammation. Interpretation should consider that results are a snapshot influenced by recent diet, medications, and illness.

Interpreting results and next steps

Use test findings to prioritize evidence-based interventions: increasing dietary fiber and plant diversity to support SCFA producers, incorporating fermented foods to encourage beneficial strains, or consulting a clinician when pathogenic markers are present. Limitations and best practices

Microbiome testing provides correlations and hypotheses rather than definitive diagnoses. Results vary with sampling timing and methodology, so interpret trends across repeat tests rather than single values. Clinically relevant decisions—such as antibiotic or immunosuppressive therapy—should always be guided by a healthcare professional. Tracking progress

Repeat testing every 6–12 months can document changes from dietary, lifestyle, or therapeutic interventions. Use consistent collection methods and consider combining microbiome data with clinical markers (inflammation, metabolic panels) to build a clearer picture of health trajectories. If you plan to use a home kit, one commonly referenced option is the microbiome test kit, which includes guidance for users on sampling and follow-up interpretation.

In sum, examining your microbiome is a practical step for evidence-based self-monitoring when paired with cautious interpretation and professional input. The combination of appropriate testing, informed interpretation, and measured interventions supports better-informed decisions about gut health.
