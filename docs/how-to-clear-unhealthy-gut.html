An unhealthy gut—often described as dysbiosis or increased intestinal permeability—can affect digestion, immunity, energy, and even mood. Clearing an unhealthy gut is best approached as a stepwise, evidence-informed process: assess, reduce drivers of imbalance, support repair, and monitor recovery. Microbiome testing and careful lifestyle adjustments help target interventions rather than relying on one-size-fits-all approaches.

Assess: Identify imbalances with objective testing

Begin by gathering objective data. Stool-based microbiome testing can reveal microbial diversity, presence of pathogens, inflammatory markers, and metabolites such as short-chain fatty acids. Personalized reports help prioritize whether to focus on pathogen eradication, increasing beneficial taxa, or repairing the mucosal barrier. For practical guidance on a structured approach, consult this guide on how to clear an unhealthy gut.

Reduce drivers of dysbiosis

Address common contributors: minimize processed foods and excess added sugars, review recent or frequent antibiotic exposure with your clinician, and reduce chronic stress where possible. Targeted antimicrobial approaches—whether botanical agents, short courses of prescription antimicrobials, or dietary exclusion—should be chosen based on testing results to avoid unnecessary disruption of beneficial microbes.

Support repair and repopulation

  • Dietary strategies: Emphasize whole foods, a variety of fibers, and polyphenol-rich plants to feed beneficial microbes. Reintroduce fermentable fibers gradually if sensitivity is present.
  • Probiotics and prebiotics: Use strains supported by evidence for your condition and add prebiotic fibers to encourage short-chain fatty acid production, especially butyrate, which supports colonic health.
  • Nutritional support for barrier repair: Nutrients like L-glutamine, zinc, and collagen peptides are commonly used to support enterocyte function and tight junction integrity under clinical supervision.
  • Targeted postbiotics: Supplementation or dietary strategies that increase beneficial metabolites (e.g., butyrate precursors, resistant starch) help create a repair-friendly environment.

Detoxification and safety considerations

When microbial overgrowth or pathogens are present, a carefully monitored detox or antimicrobial strategy can be useful. Avoid extreme cleansing regimens; use binders or hepatic-supportive nutrients only when indicated. Testing-informed plans reduce the risk of adverse reactions such as Herxheimer-type die-off.

Lifestyle and long-term resilience

Sleep, stress management, and regular physical activity shape the gut-brain axis and microbial diversity. Time-restricted eating and consistent daily routines also influence circadian-linked microbial behavior. Periodic re-testing helps assess progress and tailor ongoing strategies—see an analysis of insurance considerations for testing on coverage and testing logistics.

If you travel frequently, be aware travel-related factors can transiently alter lab results; learn more about travel effects on sampling and interpretation and practical steps to minimize variability.
