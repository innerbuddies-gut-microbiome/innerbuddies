If you experience persistent bloating, food sensitivities, fatigue, or skin problems, increased intestinal permeability—commonly called leaky gut—may be a contributing factor. Leaky gut occurs when tight junctions between intestinal cells loosen, allowing microbial products and undigested particles to enter circulation and trigger inflammation. Addressing this condition is most effective when guided by objective measures of gut health and evidence-based interventions.

Role of the gut microbiome and testing

The gut microbiome plays a central role in maintaining the intestinal barrier. Beneficial bacteria produce short-chain fatty acids like butyrate, which help nourish colonocytes and preserve tight junctions, while dysbiosis can promote inflammation and permeability. Microbiome testing clarifies which microbial imbalances may be contributing to symptoms. Many people find targeted testing useful to prioritize dietary changes, prebiotic and probiotic strategies, and selective supplements. For an overview of practical steps and a personalized testing pathway, see this guide on how to heal a leaky gut.

Dietary principles for intestinal repair

A gut-healing diet emphasizes whole foods that support microbial diversity and reduce inflammatory stimuli. Prioritize prebiotic-rich vegetables (onion, garlic, leeks), diverse fiber sources, polyphenol-rich fruits, omega-3 fatty acids, and moderate amounts of fermented foods if tolerated. Reduce refined sugars, ultra-processed foods, and known personal triggers such as gluten or dairy when sensitivity is suspected. Bone broth, collagen-containing foods, and glutamine may support mucosal recovery in some people.

Lifestyle and targeted interventions

Non-dietary factors strongly influence intestinal permeability. Chronic stress, poor sleep, and sedentary behavior can worsen dysbiosis and barrier function. Practices that support recovery include regular sleep hygiene, stress reduction techniques (mindfulness, slow breathing), daily movement, and maintaining hydration. When guided by testing, targeted supplements such as L-glutamine, zinc carnosine, specific probiotic strains, and butyrate may be appropriate to accelerate repair. Antimicrobials or antifungals should be considered only when testing indicates overgrowth and under professional supervision.

Monitoring progress and related resources

Objective retesting every 3–6 months helps determine whether microbiome-directed interventions are shifting microbial composition and reducing markers associated with permeability. Keeping a symptom diary alongside test results clarifies which changes correlate with improvement. Working with a clinician or qualified practitioner to interpret results and tailor interventions reduces the risk of inappropriate or counterproductive steps.

In summary, healing a leaky gut typically requires a combined approach: targeted dietary adjustments, lifestyle optimization, evidence-based supplements when indicated, and objective microbiome assessment to guide and monitor progress. With measured, data-informed steps, many people see meaningful improvements in symptoms and intestinal barrier function over months rather than weeks.
