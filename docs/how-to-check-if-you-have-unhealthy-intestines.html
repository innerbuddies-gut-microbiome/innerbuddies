How to check if I have unhealthy intestines?

Unhealthy intestines can influence digestion, immunity, energy levels, and mood. Identifying intestinal problems early helps guide appropriate evaluation and targeted interventions. This article outlines common signs of gut imbalance, practical assessment steps, and the role of microbiome testing and targeted diagnostics in clarifying whether your intestines are functioning suboptimally.

Common signs that suggest intestinal imbalance

Many symptoms point to compromised intestinal health; none are definitive on their own. Key red flags include persistent bloating, chronic constipation or diarrhea, unexplained fatigue, recurrent infections or inflammatory skin conditions, new food sensitivities, and cognitive symptoms such as brain fog or mood changes. Acute symptoms such as fever, severe abdominal pain, visible blood in stool, or rapid weight loss warrant urgent medical evaluation.

How to assess your gut: stepwise approach

1) Track symptoms and patterns — document timing, triggers (foods, stress), stool form, and associated signs like fever or weight change. 2) Review recent exposures — antibiotic use, international travel, or foodborne illness can leave lingering effects on the microbiome. 3) Basic labs and clinical exam — primary care providers may order blood work, inflammatory markers, or refer for imaging or endoscopy if structural disease is suspected. 4) Consider specific functional testing when symptoms persist despite routine evaluation.

Role of microbiome and targeted testing

Advances in gut microbiome analysis can reveal microbial diversity, presence of potentially pathogenic organisms, and functional markers such as short-chain fatty acid production or inflammatory signals. Stool-based testing (including 16S rRNA or metagenomic sequencing) can provide a snapshot of the intestinal microbial ecosystem and suggest contributors to symptoms like SIBO, dysbiosis, or post-infectious changes. When to suspect leaky gut or infection

Increased intestinal permeability (“leaky gut”) is associated with new food sensitivities, systemic inflammation, and some autoimmune symptoms. Pattern recognition from symptoms plus microbiome markers (for example, reduced mucin-producing species or butyrate producers) can raise suspicion. If infection is possible—severe cramping, bloody stools, high fever—targeted pathogen testing (PCR panels, cultures) is appropriate.

Interpreting results and next steps

Laboratory and microbiome results should be interpreted in the context of clinical history. Reports that show reduced diversity, overrepresentation of inflammatory taxa, or low levels of butyrate producers provide mechanistic clues but do not replace clinical judgment. Evidence-based strategies typically focus on diet quality, measured supplementation, stress reduction, and, when indicated, targeted antimicrobials or probiotics under clinical supervision.
