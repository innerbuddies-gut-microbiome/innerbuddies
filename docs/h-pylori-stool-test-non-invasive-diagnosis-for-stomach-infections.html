Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) is a common bacterium that can colonize the stomach lining and contribute to gastritis, peptic ulcers, and, in some cases, an increased long-term risk of gastric cancer. Because many infections are asymptomatic, reliable testing is important for identifying active infection and guiding appropriate management. The [H. pylori stool test](https://www.innerbuddies.com/blogs/gut-health/h-pylori-stool-test-non-invasive-diagnosis-for-stomach-infections) offers a non-invasive way to detect current infection by identifying bacterial antigens shed in feces. How the stool antigen test works The stool antigen test detects proteins (antigens) produced by H. pylori in a small sample of stool. Laboratory assays—typically enzyme immunoassays—identify these antigens and indicate active infection. Because antigen detection reflects current bacterial presence rather than past exposure, this test is useful both for initial diagnosis and for confirming eradication after treatment, provided timing and medication effects are considered. When to consider testing Testing is advised for individuals with persistent upper abdominal pain, unexplained dyspepsia (indigestion), recurrent nausea, bloating, or a history of peptic ulcer disease. Testing may also be reasonable for patients with a family history of gastric cancer or when symptoms persist despite empiric management. Non-invasive testing is particularly advantageous for children and for patients who prefer to avoid endoscopy. Advantages and limitations Advantages of the stool antigen test include its non-invasive nature, good accuracy for detecting active infection, and suitability for home sample collection. It is generally less expensive and less resource-intensive than endoscopy. Limitations include potential false-negative results if the patient has recently taken antibiotics, bismuth compounds, or proton pump inhibitors (PPIs). To minimize false negatives, testing should typically be performed at least two weeks after stopping antibiotics and bismuth, and ideally two to four weeks after discontinuing PPIs when clinically appropriate. Test sensitivity and specificity can vary by assay and laboratory; clinicians interpret results alongside clinical presentation. Role in treatment and follow-up When an active H. pylori infection is confirmed, standard management typically involves a combination of antibiotics and acid-suppressing medications tailored to local resistance patterns and individual patient factors. Follow-up testing to confirm eradication is important and the stool antigen test is commonly used for this purpose once an appropriate interval has passed after therapy.
