What Are the Symptoms of Unhealthy Intestines?

Unhealthy intestines can manifest through a range of digestive and systemic symptoms that signal an imbalance in the gut ecosystem. Recognizing these signs early can guide evaluations and evidence-based interventions. Common presentations include bloating, altered bowel habits, food sensitivities, persistent fatigue, cognitive changes, and skin issues.

Digestive and bowel symptoms

Direct indicators of intestinal dysfunction are often gastrointestinal: bloating, excessive gas, heartburn, abdominal pain, constipation, diarrhea, or alternating bowel habits. These symptoms can arise from microbial imbalances such as small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), pathogens, or reduced diversity of beneficial bacteria. Altered microbial fermentation may increase gas production and slow or accelerate transit time, producing discomfort and nutrient malabsorption.

Food sensitivities, cravings, and appetite changes

An imbalanced gut can contribute to new food sensitivities and persistent cravings. Increased intestinal permeability (so-called "leaky gut") may allow food particles to interact with the immune system and promote inflammatory responses, perceived as sensitivity. Some microbes influence the gut–brain axis and can alter appetite-regulating hormones and reward pathways, which may explain persistent sugar cravings or changes in hunger.

Systemic signs: fatigue, brain fog, and skin conditions

Beyond digestion, the gut affects nutrient absorption, immune signaling, and neurochemical production. Chronic fatigue can follow from poor absorption of B12, iron, or other micronutrients, while systemic inflammation—driven by microbial products like lipopolysaccharide (LPS)—can impair sleep and energy metabolism. Cognitive symptoms such as brain fog may reflect immune-mediated neuroinflammation or altered neurotransmitter production in the gut. Dermatological manifestations (acne, eczema, rosacea) are increasingly linked to the gut–skin axis and systemic inflammatory mediators.

When testing helps clarify causes

Stool-based microbial analysis can provide insight into community diversity, presence of pathogenic organisms, and markers associated with inflammation or intestinal barrier dysfunction.
