The gut microbiota is the community of microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, archaea and fungi — that live in the human digestive tract. These microbes, collectively called the gut microbiome when considering their genes, are abundant: contemporary estimates suggest trillions of microbes predominantly inhabit the gut and can influence metabolism, immunity, digestion and even mood. Each person typically hosts a few hundred bacterial species drawn from a potential pool of roughly a thousand species found across human populations. Gut microorganisms perform multiple essential functions. They help break down dietary components that human enzymes cannot digest, produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) such as acetate, propionate and butyrate that support colonocyte energy and gut barrier integrity, synthesize certain vitamins, and competitively inhibit pathogenic microbes. The genetic repertoire of the microbiome far exceeds that of human cells, which helps explain why microbial activity complements human physiology in diverse ways. Microbiota composition is dynamic across the lifespan. Colonization begins at birth and is shaped by early factors such as delivery mode (vaginal versus cesarean), breastfeeding, early antibiotic exposure and environmental contacts. Later influences include diet diversity, physical activity, living environment and contact with pets. Greater microbial diversity is generally associated with resilience and health, while reduced diversity or pronounced imbalances (dysbiosis) are linked in research to conditions ranging from functional gut disorders to metabolic and inflammatory states. Diet is one of the most modifiable and impactful factors for the gut ecosystem. Fibers and other compounds that resist digestion in the upper gut reach the large intestine where microbes ferment them into beneficial metabolites. Vegetables, whole grains and fermented foods can support populations of beneficial bacteria such as Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus. Conversely, diets high in ultra-processed foods and excess sugars can favor less desirable microbial configurations. Assessing the gut microbiota through stool-based testing (microbiome analysis) can reveal information about microbial diversity, the relative abundance of key taxa, and functional potential when sequenced at higher resolution (e.g., 16S rRNA gene sequencing or metagenomics). Such analyses can inform personalized dietary adjustments, guide clinical evaluation of persistent digestive symptoms, or help researchers study links between microbes and health outcomes. Interpretation is evolving: results should be considered alongside clinical context, laboratory data and evidence-based recommendations rather than as standalone diagnoses.
