Can Gut Microbiome Tests Help With Skin Problems?

Emerging research supports a link between gastrointestinal microbial composition and common dermatologic conditions such as acne, eczema and psoriasis. A gut microbiome test analyzes the diversity and functional capacity of microbes in the digestive tract, offering data that can clarify whether dysbiosis, increased intestinal permeability, or impaired nutrient metabolism may be contributing to skin inflammation.

How the gut and skin communicate

The gut-skin axis describes bidirectional communication involving immune signaling, metabolic byproducts and neuroendocrine pathways. For example, a disrupted gut microbiome can promote systemic inflammation via lipopolysaccharides (LPS) and pro-inflammatory cytokines, while decreased abundance of short-chain fatty acid producers can impair barrier integrity. These mechanisms help explain why gastrointestinal dysfunction and altered microbial profiles are frequently observed in people with inflammatory skin conditions.

What microbiome testing can reveal

Modern tests report measures such as microbial diversity, relative abundance of key taxa, presence of pathogens and inferred metabolic pathways (e.g., butyrate synthesis, vitamin biosynthesis). Interpreting these results in context—alongside symptoms, diet and medication history—can identify patterns associated with skin issues, such as low levels of anti-inflammatory taxa or enrichment of pathobionts linked to permeability.

Practical implications for acne and inflammatory skin disease

In acne, gut-derived inflammation and hormonal dysregulation (including effects on insulin-like growth factor signaling) may worsen sebum production and lesion formation. A targeted microbiome profile can suggest nutritional or probiotic strategies aimed at restoring SCFA producers and supporting barrier function. Clinical literature also notes associations between small intestinal bacterial overgrowth and rosacea, and between gut dysbiosis and atopic dermatitis severity.

Using tests responsibly

Microbiome data are most useful when combined with clinical evaluation. Tests are not diagnostic of specific skin diseases on their own, but they can highlight modifiable contributors—such as low microbial diversity, indications of poor fiber fermentation, or taxa associated with inflammation. Is microbiome testing right for you?

Testing can be informative for individuals with persistent or treatment-resistant skin conditions who also have gastrointestinal symptoms, frequent antibiotic exposure or suspected food intolerances.
