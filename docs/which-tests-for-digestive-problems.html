Digestive symptoms such as bloating, constipation, diarrhea, acid reflux, and abdominal pain are common and can have diverse causes. Accurate diagnosis generally requires a combination of structural, biochemical, and functional assessments. This article summarizes primary diagnostic options—including stool testing, endoscopic procedures, imaging, and liver function tests—and explains where gut microbiome analysis fits within a clinical evaluation.

Common diagnostic categories

Diagnostic approaches for digestive complaints typically fall into four categories: stool-based tests, endoscopy (upper and lower), imaging and blood tests (including liver panels), and functional or molecular assays such as microbiome sequencing. Each provides different information, and the choice depends on symptoms, red flags, and clinical history.

Stool analysis

Stool testing is useful for detecting infections, inflammation, and broad ecosystem changes. Culture-based tests and PCR pathogen panels identify bacterial, viral, or parasitic causes of acute diarrhea. Fecal inflammatory markers (e.g., calprotectin) help distinguish inflammatory bowel disease from functional disorders. Comprehensive stool microbiome panels assess diversity, keystone species, and metabolic markers (like short-chain fatty acids), which can inform dietary or probiotic strategies.

Gastroscopy and colonoscopy

Endoscopic visualization remains the gold standard for structural abnormalities. Gastroscopy (upper endoscopy) inspects the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum and can diagnose ulcers, gastritis, celiac disease, and H. pylori infection via biopsy. Colonoscopy evaluates the colon for polyps, tumors, and inflammatory changes; biopsies provide histology and optional tissue-based microbial sampling. These procedures are indicated when alarm features (bleeding, weight loss, anemia) or age-based screening criteria are present.

Imaging and liver function tests

Cross-sectional imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI) is used to assess structural causes outside the bowel—such as gallbladder disease, pancreatitis, or masses. Liver function tests and pancreatic enzymes evaluate hepatobiliary and exocrine pancreatic contributions to symptoms like jaundice, upper abdominal pain, or malabsorption.

Where microbiome testing fits

Microbiome analysis complements, rather than replaces, traditional diagnostics. It provides insight into microbial composition, diversity, and functional potential that may underlie chronic or functional GI disorders (e.g., IBS, suspected SIBO patterns, post-antibiotic dysbiosis). Because stool sampling mainly reflects distal colonic communities, microbiome results are most useful when interpreted alongside symptoms, imaging, endoscopy, and lab results.
