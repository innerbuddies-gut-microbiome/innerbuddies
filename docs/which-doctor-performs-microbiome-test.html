A microbiome test analyzes the community of microorganisms living in the gut and can inform clinical and lifestyle decisions. Several types of healthcare professionals and service providers can order, perform, or interpret these tests. Understanding the differences between them helps you choose appropriate expertise for diagnostic clarity and follow-up care.

Gastroenterologists are specialists in digestive diseases and often coordinate microbiome testing when symptoms suggest an organic or structural issue. Because they combine clinical examination with imaging, endoscopy, and laboratory results, gastroenterologists are well placed to interpret microbiome data in the context of conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease, chronic diarrhea, or unexplained weight loss.

Functional medicine practitioners and integrative medicine doctors typically approach the microbiome from a systems perspective, exploring diet, lifestyle, hormones, and environmental contributors to dysbiosis. These clinicians may use sequencing-based tests alongside nutritional interventions, targeted supplements, and behavior change strategies to address root causes. Their practice style emphasizes longitudinal monitoring and individualized plans rather than single-point symptom management.

Clinical nutritionists and dietitians with training in gut health can translate microbiome findings into practical dietary recommendations. They focus on fiber patterns, prebiotic and probiotic food sources, elimination diets for suspected food sensitivities, and strategies to support microbial diversity. Nutrition professionals often collaborate with medical providers to ensure recommendations align with any medical diagnoses or medications.

Microbiome analysis providers—both clinical laboratories and accredited research services—perform the sequencing and bioinformatic interpretation. Some labs work directly with clinicians, while others offer direct-to-consumer kits that allow individuals to collect stool samples at home. Laboratory reports vary in scope: some provide taxonomic profiles and diversity metrics, while others include functional predictions or metabolite measurements. It’s important to review what a given test measures before selecting it.

Primary care physicians can initiate basic microbiome testing to screen for dysbiosis when symptoms are non-specific, but they may refer patients to specialists for complex interpretation or procedural evaluation. Referral is prudent when test results suggest pathogenic organisms, when symptoms are severe, or when additional diagnostic procedures (e.g., colonoscopy) are indicated.

Evidence-based use of microbiome testing remains an evolving area. Tests can reveal associations between microbial patterns and symptoms, but causation is often unclear. Clinicians combine test results with clinical history, labs, and imaging to formulate a management plan. Selecting the right professional depends on symptom severity, the need for diagnostic procedures, and whether your goals are diagnostic, therapeutic, or preventive.
