Microbiome diversity describes the variety and relative abundance of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, viruses and archaea — that occupy a particular environment, most notably the human gut. A diverse community tends to be more resilient and functionally versatile, supporting nutrient processing, immune modulation and resistance to pathogen invasion.

Measuring diversity involves more than counting species. Ecologists distinguish alpha diversity (diversity within a single sample) from beta diversity (differences between samples). Common alpha metrics include richness and the Shannon Index, while beta diversity metrics—such as Bray–Curtis dissimilarity, Jaccard index and UniFrac—help compare community composition across hosts, sites or time points.

The Shannon Index integrates both richness and evenness: it increases when more species are present and when their abundances are more evenly distributed. Because it is sensitive to changes in both rare and common taxa, the Shannon Index is frequently used in gut microbiome studies to summarize community complexity and to detect perturbations associated with diet, medication or disease.

Beta diversity measures complement alpha metrics by revealing how communities differ. For example, Bray–Curtis quantifies compositional differences based on abundance data, while Jaccard focuses on shared presence/absence. Phylogenetic measures such as UniFrac add evolutionary context, weighting differences by how distantly related the taxa are. Together, these tools allow researchers to track shifts linked to interventions, geographic factors or host phenotype.

Loss of diversity, or dysbiosis, has been associated with a range of health conditions. Observational and mechanistic studies link reduced gut diversity to inflammatory bowel disease, metabolic disorders and altered immune responses. Common drivers of dysbiosis include broad-spectrum antibiotic exposure, low-fiber diets, chronic stress and certain infections. Restoring diversity is a multifaceted task: dietary fiber and plant-rich diets promote fermentative bacteria, prebiotics selectively feed beneficial taxa, and some clinical contexts may consider targeted microbial therapeutics under specialist guidance.

Interpreting diversity measures requires caution. Differences in sampling, sequencing methodology, and data processing can influence reported diversity values. Comparisons are most reliable when methods are standardized and appropriate statistical controls are applied. Longitudinal sampling and multi-metric approaches (combining alpha, beta and functional analyses) provide a more complete picture of ecosystem dynamics than any single index alone.

For researchers and clinicians, integrating diversity metrics with functional readouts—metabolomics, immune markers or clinical outcomes—helps bridge descriptions of community structure to host physiology.
