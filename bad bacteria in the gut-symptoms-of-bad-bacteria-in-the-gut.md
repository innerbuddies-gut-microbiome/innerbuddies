# What are the symptoms of bad bacteria in the gut?

The gut contains a complex community of microorganisms that support digestion, immunity, and even mood regulation. When this balance shifts toward harmful or overabundant bacteria — a state called dysbiosis — a variety of digestive and systemic symptoms can appear. Understanding these signs helps identify when deeper testing or clinical evaluation is warranted.

## Common symptoms to watch for

- Bloating and excessive gas: Fermentation of undigested carbohydrates by excess bacteria often produces hydrogen, methane, or sulfur gases, causing post-meal bloating and discomfort.
- Irregular bowel movements: Persistent diarrhea, constipation, or alternating stool patterns may indicate bacterial interference with motility or water absorption.
- Abdominal pain and cramping: Localized cramping, urgency, or persistent lower abdominal pain can signal inflammation or pathogenic activity in the gut lining.
- Fatigue and nutrient deficiencies: Dysbiosis can impair absorption of vitamins (e.g., B12) and minerals (e.g., iron), contributing to chronic tiredness and low energy.
- Food intolerances and cravings: New intolerances, especially to FODMAPs, or strong sugar cravings are often linked to microbial imbalances.
- Skin and systemic signs: Conditions like eczema, unexplained weight change, and mood disturbances (brain fog, anxiety) have been associated with gut microbiome shifts.

## When bacterial overgrowth is likely

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) is a clear example where bacteria normally confined to the colon proliferate in the small intestine. Typical indicators include severe bloating after meals, foul-smelling gas, belching, nausea, and nutrient malabsorption. Breath testing (hydrogen and methane) is the common diagnostic tool for SIBO, while stool-based sequencing can clarify broader colonic composition.

## Inflammation and infection

Certain pathogens (for example, species of Salmonella or Clostridium) produce toxins that damage the mucosal barrier and trigger immune responses. Chronic intestinal inflammation may present as persistent diarrhea, mucus or blood in stool, fever during acute flares, and progressive weight loss. Over time, sustained inflammation can impair intestinal villi and lead to malabsorption and systemic consequences.

## Summary

Symptoms of harmful bacterial overgrowth range from digestive discomfort (bloating, altered stools) to systemic effects (fatigue, nutrient deficiencies, skin issues). When symptoms are persistent or progressive, targeted testing can clarify microbial contributors and guide evidence-based interventions without relying on symptomatic treatments alone.
