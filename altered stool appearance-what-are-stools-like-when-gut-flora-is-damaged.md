Understanding changes in stool can offer direct insight into gut microbial health. A damaged or imbalanced gut flora (dysbiosis) often alters stool color, consistency, odor, and composition, and these observable features can guide further investigation and testing.

Healthy stools are generally medium to dark brown, formed like a smooth log, and only mildly odorous. Beneficial bacteria such as Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium support fiber fermentation, short-chain fatty acid production, and water balance — processes that maintain normal stool appearance and transit. When the microbiome is disrupted by antibiotics, poor diet, stress, or infection, several characteristic stool changes commonly appear.

Color and visual cues
- Pale, yellow, or gray stools may signal impaired bile metabolism or rapid transit, both of which can be influenced by microbial imbalance. 
- Green stools can result from rapid transit or changes in bile processing; very dark or black stools warrant clinical evaluation for bleeding or iron-related metabolism.
- Visible mucus, oily sheen, or undigested material suggests malabsorption or inflammation linked to shifts in bacterial enzyme activity.

Consistency and frequency
- Persistent hard lumps or Type 1–2 on the Bristol Stool Chart often relate to low microbial diversity, reduced fiber fermentation, or methane-producing organisms that slow transit.
- Loose, watery stools (Types 6–7) are frequently associated with overgrowth of aggressive bacteria, viral or fungal disturbances, lactose intolerance, or bile acid malabsorption.
- Fluctuating stool types or chronic irregularity suggest an unstable microbial ecosystem and are worth clinical attention.

Odor and biochemical signs
A foul, sulfurous odor often arises from proteolytic fermentation producing hydrogen sulfide and ammonia. These by-products reflect altered protein metabolism and an overrepresentation of putrefactive bacteria. Biochemical testing of stool can detect elevated pH, reduced short-chain fatty acids (especially butyrate), and markers of inflammation or malabsorption.

Clinical relevance and testing
Persistent or unexplained stool changes are not diagnostic by appearance alone but are important signals. A targeted stool or microbiome analysis can quantify diversity, identify pathogenic overgrowths (including small intestinal bacterial overgrowth patterns), measure metabolic outputs (SCFAs, bile acids), and flag inflammation.
