The microbiome is the collective community of microorganisms and their genetic material that live on and inside the human body. Most research focuses on the gut microbiome because it hosts the largest and most diverse microbial population, but microbes are present on the skin, in the mouth, and throughout the respiratory and urogenital tracts. Understanding the microbiome means considering both the species present (microbiota) and the functions those organisms perform in concert.

At a functional level, the gut microbiome contributes to digestion, nutrient extraction, vitamin synthesis, and immune system education. Short-chain fatty acids produced by microbial fermentation of dietary fiber, for example, support colonocyte health and modulate systemic inflammation. These tests can estimate diversity, relative abundance of taxa, and markers associated with inflammation or dysbiosis. Measuring diversity is one common objective of testing. Higher microbial richness and evenness are often associated with better health outcomes and greater ecological resilience; lower diversity is correlated in many studies with conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease, obesity, and metabolic dysfunction. However, interpretation requires context: geographic, dietary, age-related, and medication-related factors all shape what is a 'normal' or desirable profile for an individual.

Modifiable factors that influence the microbiome include diet, antibiotic exposure, stress, sleep, and physical activity. Diets rich in varied fibers, polyphenols, and minimally processed plant foods tend to support diverse and functionally robust microbial communities. Fermented foods and specific prebiotic fibers can selectively promote beneficial taxa, while repeated or broad-spectrum antibiotic courses can reduce richness and allow opportunistic organisms to occupy ecological niches.

Its composition and function reflect lifetime exposures and current behaviors, and while testing can provide informative snapshots, results are most useful when interpreted alongside clinical information and reproducible lifestyle strategies that support microbial diversity and function.
