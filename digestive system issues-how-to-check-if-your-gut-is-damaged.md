Your gut plays a central role in digestion, immunity, and brain function. When the intestinal lining or microbial community is disrupted, symptoms can appear both in the digestive tract and systemically. This article outlines common signs of gut damage, diagnostic approaches, and practical steps to assess intestinal health in an evidence-based, neutral style.

Understanding gut injury begins with recognizing symptomatic patterns. Typical digestive complaints—bloating, gas, diarrhea, constipation, and reflux—can indicate dysbiosis (microbial imbalance), inflammation, or motility changes. Beyond these, non-digestive signals such as chronic fatigue, brain fog, skin disorders (eczema, acne, psoriasis), unexplained weight changes, and mood disturbances often correlate with altered gut function and intestinal permeability.

Diagnostic tools that help determine whether the gut is damaged include stool-based microbiome analysis, blood markers, breath tests, and metabolite profiling. Stool sequencing (16S rRNA or whole-genome shotgun) reveals microbial diversity and the presence of potentially harmful species; functional stool panels can show inflammation markers and digestive enzyme levels. Blood tests may measure systemic inflammation (CRP), nutrient deficiencies, or markers associated with intestinal permeability. Breath tests assess hydrogen and methane to detect small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). Urinary organic acid testing can highlight microbial metabolites suggestive of yeast overgrowth or dysbiosis.

Interpreting results benefits from professional input. Integrative clinicians or practitioners experienced in gastrointestinal diagnostics can correlate laboratory findings with clinical history, medication use (especially antibiotics and NSAIDs), diet, and lifestyle factors. A single abnormal finding rarely provides a complete picture; trends over time and symptom correlation are important for determining clinical relevance.

If testing indicates reduced microbial diversity, inflammation, or markers of permeability, recommended evidence-based actions typically include dietary adjustments (increasing fiber and fermented foods, reducing highly processed foods and excess sugars), targeted treatment of infections or overgrowth where indicated, and addressing factors that impair healing such as chronic stress, poor sleep, or recurrent antibiotic exposure. Supplements that support barrier repair and digestion are sometimes used under professional guidance; these decisions should follow objective test results.
