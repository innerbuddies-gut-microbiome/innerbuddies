# How can I test my gut health?

Understanding the state of your gut is a practical step toward improving digestion, immune resilience, and overall well‑being. This article summarizes common assessment methods, what they measure, and how to interpret results in an evidence‑based, clinical context.

## Symptom tracking and initial evaluation

Before ordering tests, systematic symptom tracking is valuable. Keep a food and symptom diary for several weeks to capture patterns in bloating, bowel frequency and form, heartburn, stool appearance, and systemic signs such as fatigue or skin changes. Routine blood work (CBC, metabolic panel, thyroid function, vitamin B12, iron studies) can identify systemic contributors to digestive symptoms.

## Stool and microbiome analysis

Stool testing can assess inflammation, digestion, and the composition of the gut microbial community. Common modalities include 16S rRNA sequencing for bacterial composition and metagenomic sequencing for broader microbial and functional insight. Stool markers such as calprotectin and lactoferrin indicate mucosal inflammation; fecal elastase can assess pancreatic exocrine function.

For more technical references on microbiome testing and how it relates to diet and sensitivities, consult this resource on gut microbiome tests and food sensitivities: <a href='https://innerbuddies-gut-microbiome.github.io/innerbuddies/gut-microbiome-tests-food-sensitivities'>Gut microbiome tests & food sensitivities</a>.

## Tests of intestinal barrier and mucosal immunity

When symptoms or comorbid conditions suggest increased intestinal permeability or immune dysregulation, specific assessments are available. Lactulose/mannitol permeability tests estimate translocation across the gut barrier. Zonulin assays are sometimes used as a marker of tight junction regulation, and stool secretory IgA (sIgA) provides a window into mucosal immune activity. Interpret these tests alongside clinical context, since single markers can be influenced by transient factors.

## Small intestinal and motility assessments

Suspected small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) or dysmotility is commonly evaluated with breath tests (measuring hydrogen and methane) and transit studies. Endoscopic evaluation with biopsy may be indicated when alarm features exist (unintentional weight loss, GI bleeding, persistent severe pain), to rule out structural disease or inflammatory bowel disease.

## Integrating results and follow‑up

A comprehensive approach combines symptom history, laboratory and stool markers, and targeted imaging or endoscopy when needed. Microbiome reports can inform dietary fiber, prebiotic, and probiotic strategies, while permeability and inflammation markers can guide mucosal support and anti‑inflammatory measures. Retesting after interventions helps assess response and refine the plan.
