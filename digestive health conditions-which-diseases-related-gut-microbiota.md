The human gut microbiota is a complex community of bacteria, fungi, viruses, and other microbes that influence digestion, immunity, metabolism, and brain function. Research increasingly links shifts in microbial composition—so-called dysbiosis—with a range of conditions beyond the gut. This concise review outlines major diseases associated with gut microbiota changes, key mechanisms, and how microbiome profiling can inform personalized care.

## Key conditions associated with gut microbiota changes

- Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): Altered bacterial communities, small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), and methane-producing archaea are commonly observed in people with IBS and can correlate with bloating, pain, and stool changes.

- Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis are linked to reduced microbial diversity and expansion of pro-inflammatory taxa that can exacerbate mucosal inflammation.

- Metabolic disorders (obesity, metabolic syndrome, Type 2 diabetes): Differences in microbial composition affect energy harvest, short-chain fatty acid production, and systemic inflammation; specific taxa shifts have been associated with insulin resistance.

- Cardiovascular disease: Gut-derived metabolites such as trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) originate from microbial metabolism and have been implicated in atherosclerosis risk.

- Mental health disorders: The gut–brain axis connects microbial signals to neural, endocrine, and immune pathways. Associations exist between dysbiosis and conditions like depression and anxiety.

- Autoimmune and allergic diseases: Early-life microbial imbalance and reduced regulatory taxa can impair immune tolerance, contributing to autoimmune diseases (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) and atopic disorders.

- Intestinal disorders: Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO), diverticulitis, celiac disease, and various colitides show distinct microbial signatures that influence disease course and treatment response.

- Liver and digestive conditions: Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), chronic constipation, diarrhea, GERD, and malabsorption syndromes are frequently associated with microbiota alterations.

## Mechanisms connecting microbes to disease

Dysbiosis can reduce beneficial metabolites (like butyrate), increase production of endotoxins (lipopolysaccharide), alter bile acid metabolism, and disrupt intestinal barrier integrity. These changes promote low-grade systemic inflammation, modify host metabolism, and influence immune programming. Microbial metabolites and immune interactions form plausible biological pathways linking gut communities to distant organs.

## Microbiome testing and clinical relevance

## Conclusion

Gut microbiota alterations are implicated across digestive, metabolic, immune, cardiovascular, and neurobehavioral conditions. Understanding microbial contributions is an evolving field; integrating microbiome data with clinical assessment can help prioritize interventions aimed at restoring microbial balance and reducing disease risk.
