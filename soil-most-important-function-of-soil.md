Introduction

Soil’s most important function is to act as a foundation for life: a medium that supports biological communities, cycles nutrients, and enables resilience in ecosystems. This role applies to agricultural landscapes and serves as an apt metaphor for the human gut, where the microbiome functions as an “internal soil” that shapes digestion, immunity, and systemic health.

The foundation role: nutrients, habitat, and cycling

In terrestrial ecosystems soil provides three interlinked services: it stores and supplies essential nutrients, offers a habitat and physical structure for organisms, and mediates biochemical cycling (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus). Those services sustain plant growth, regulate water and gas exchange, and support diverse microbial and animal life. When any of these components fail—through erosion, contamination, or loss of organic matter—the capacity of soil to support life is impaired.

Parallels in the gut microbiome

The gut microbiome mirrors these functions. It supplies metabolic capabilities the human host lacks (e.g., breakdown of complex polysaccharides), provides a habitat for microbial communities that defend against pathogens, and participates in nutrient and signaling molecule cycles that affect host physiology. Microbial diversity and structural integrity of the mucosal environment are analogous to soil fertility and aggregation; loss of diversity is comparable to soil degradation and correlates with conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease, metabolic syndrome, and some mood disorders.

Testing and monitoring the internal soil

Assessing soil health is routine in agriculture; similarly, characterizing the gut’s microbial composition and function can inform remediation strategies. Laboratory-based microbiome assays can quantify diversity, detect opportunistic organisms, and infer metabolic capacities (e.g., short-chain fatty acid production), offering evidence to guide dietary, behavioral, or clinical adjustments.

Management principles: fertility, structure, and conservation

Three management themes translate well between external and internal soils. Fertility emphasizes supplying substrates and cofactors (dietary fiber, micronutrients) to sustain beneficial microbes. Structure involves preserving mucosal barriers and microbial biofilms that promote stability. Conservation focuses on preventing perturbations—mindful antibiotic use, minimizing exposure to gut irritants, and adopting consistent dietary patterns rather than extreme short-term changes.

Ecosystem perspective and integrative approaches

Viewing the gut as an ecosystem clarifies why single interventions (e.g., a single probiotic strain) may have limited, transient effects. Ecosystem-level strategies—diverse prebiotic and fermented foods, stress reduction, and lifestyle consistency—aim to restore network interactions among bacteria, archaea, fungi, and bacteriophages. Conclusion

The single most important function of soil—supporting and cycling life—applies equally to the gut microbiome. An evidence-based, ecosystem-oriented approach to assessment and management helps maintain the internal soil’s fertility, structure, and conservation, thereby supporting host health.
