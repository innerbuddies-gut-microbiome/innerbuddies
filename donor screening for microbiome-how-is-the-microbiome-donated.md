Microbiome donation refers to the transfer of a healthy person’s gut microbial community to another individual to restore balance and function. Advances in sequencing-based gut microbiome testing have clarified which microbial features matter for health, and clinical protocols—centered on fecal microbiota transfer (FMT)—have formalized how donations are collected, screened, and administered.

## Understanding microbiome donation and testing

Assessment begins with detailed microbial profiling. Clinicians and researchers use stool-based assays to measure diversity, the abundance of key taxa, and the presence of pathogenic or antibiotic-resistance genes. ## Fecal microbiota transfer (FMT) basics

FMT is the principal therapeutic route for microbiome donation. It involves preparing donor stool into a filtered, preserved suspension or encapsulated form that can be delivered by colonoscopy, enema, nasoenteric tube, or oral capsules. FMT is best-established for treating recurrent Clostridioides difficile infection, where it has shown high efficacy in randomized and observational studies. Emerging research is evaluating FMT and donor-derived products for inflammatory bowel disease, metabolic disorders, and modulation of the gut–brain axis, though evidence is still evolving for these indications.

## Donor screening and laboratory safeguards

Robust donor screening minimizes risk. The screening pathway typically includes a medical and lifestyle questionnaire, clinical evaluation, serologic and stool pathogen testing (bacterial, viral, parasitic), assessment for multi-drug resistant organisms, and metagenomic or targeted sequencing to evaluate microbiome diversity and absence of deleterious taxa. Samples are processed in controlled laboratory settings: homogenized, filtered, mixed with cryoprotectants when needed, aliquoted, and stored under ultra-low temperatures to preserve viability.

## Practical considerations and monitoring

Recipients often undergo bowel preparation or short antibiotic regimens before transplantation to reduce microbial competition. Post-transplant follow-up includes clinical assessment and repeat microbiome testing to document engraftment, durability, and any adverse events. Ethical practices require informed consent, data privacy protections, and oversight from institutional review boards when donations are used in research or clinical care.

In summary, microbiome donation is a regulated, evidence-driven process that integrates advanced testing, careful donor screening, and controlled delivery methods. As the field matures, standardized protocols and biobanking may expand therapeutic options while preserving safety and scientific rigor.
