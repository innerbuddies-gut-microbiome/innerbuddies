## Common signs that suggest gut damage

Damage or imbalance in the gut microbiome often presents beyond simple stomach upset. Typical symptoms include persistent bloating, irregular bowel movements (chronic constipation or diarrhea), unexplained fatigue, brain fog, new food sensitivities, recurrent infections, and skin conditions such as eczema or rosacea. These manifestations result from dysbiosis — a loss of microbial diversity or overgrowth of potentially harmful organisms — and from impaired gut barrier function.

## What microbiome testing reveals

Non-invasive stool-based microbiome tests can profile bacterial diversity, identify overrepresented pathogens, and quantify beneficial taxa (for example, Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium). They may also report on functional outputs such as short-chain fatty acid production and enzyme activities linked to digestion and absorption. A balanced, diverse microbiome typically supports nutrient extraction, mucosal integrity, and immune tolerance; deviations from that state can point toward underlying problems.

Neutral, evidence-based test results are useful when paired with symptoms and other laboratory markers. For example, elevated methane or certain archaea correlate with slowed transit and constipation, while increases in Proteobacteria are often associated with inflammatory states.

## Detecting leaky gut and inflammation

Increased intestinal permeability (“leaky gut”) lets antigens and bacterial products cross the epithelial barrier, potentially triggering systemic inflammation and immune responses. Microbiome tests can signal risk via low levels of mucus-supporting bacteria such as Akkermansia or by high counts of endotoxin-producing species. Complementary assays (zonulin, LPS-binding protein, fecal calprotectin) help confirm permeability or active intestinal inflammation.

Fecal inflammatory markers like calprotectin and lactoferrin indicate neutrophil activity in the gut and are commonly used to flag inflammatory bowel disease versus functional disorders. When microbial profiles show reduced SCFA-producing bacteria and elevated Enterobacteriaceae, clinicians often interpret this as a pro-inflammatory signature requiring further evaluation.

## Interpreting results and next steps

Interpreting microbiome data is most effective when combined with clinical history, diet, medication use (notably antibiotics), and symptom patterns. Targeted interventions—such as dietary adjustments to increase fermentable fiber, strategies to reduce specific overgrowths, or therapies aimed at restoring barrier function—are guided by specific findings rather than one-size-fits-all advice.
