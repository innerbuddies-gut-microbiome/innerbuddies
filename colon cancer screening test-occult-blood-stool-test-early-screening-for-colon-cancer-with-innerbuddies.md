# Occult Blood Test in Stool: Early Screening for Colon Cancer

Detecting hidden blood in stool is a widely used method for early colorectal cancer screening. The fecal occult blood test (FOBT) identifies blood that is not visible to the naked eye and can flag lesions or polyps that may bleed intermittently. Early detection through screening improves diagnostic options and clinical outcomes.

## Understanding the occult blood stool test

FOBT refers to tests that detect blood in stool samples collected at home. The traditional guaiac-based test (gFOBT) uses a chemical reaction to indicate the presence of blood, while immunochemical tests (iFOBT or FIT) use antibodies that specifically detect human hemoglobin. FITs are generally more specific to lower gastrointestinal bleeding and less affected by diet than gFOBT.

## Why early screening matters

Colorectal cancer often begins as small, noncancerous polyps that can progress over years. Many people with early-stage disease have no symptoms, so population-based screening programs aim to identify disease before clinical signs appear. A positive FOBT does not confirm cancer but indicates the need for further diagnostic evaluation, commonly colonoscopy, to visualize the colon and obtain biopsies when necessary.

## How the test is performed

Most FOBT kits include simple instructions and supplies for collecting small stool samples at home. Patients should follow sample collection guidance and any pre-test restrictions recommended by their clinician, such as avoiding certain medications or foods if using gFOBT. Samples are returned to a laboratory or analyzed with a home kit depending on the test type. Labs test for hemoglobin or induce a colorimetric reaction depending on the method used.

## Types of tests and interpretation

- gFOBT: Affordable and long-established, but can yield false positives from dietary peroxidases (e.g., some vegetables) or red meat. Several samples over consecutive bowel movements are often recommended.
- FIT/iFOBT: More specific for human blood, usually requires fewer samples, and is less influenced by diet.

A negative result reduces the likelihood of bleeding lesions but does not eliminate cancer risk. A positive result requires timely follow-up with diagnostic procedures to determine the source of bleeding.

## Who should be screened and how often

Screening guidelines vary, but many organizations recommend beginning routine screening at age 45 to 50 for average-risk adults. People with a family history of colorectal cancer, certain hereditary conditions, or prior polyps may require earlier or more frequent screening. FOBT-based strategies are often offered annually or biennially, while colonoscopy intervals differ based on findings and risk.
