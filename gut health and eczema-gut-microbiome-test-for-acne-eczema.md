Understanding whether internal microbial balance influences skin disease is an active area of research. A gut microbiome test can reveal patterns of bacterial diversity, key taxa abundance, and functional markers that are associated with inflammation, immune regulation, and barrier integrity—factors implicated in acne and eczema. For an example of a focused assessment, consider the [gut microbiome test for acne and eczema](https://www.innerbuddies.com/blogs/gut-health/gut-microbiome-test-for-acne-eczema) that links gut-derived insights to skin-specific recommendations.

Why it matters: the gut and skin communicate through immune, metabolic, and endocrine pathways. Dysbiosis—reduced diversity or overrepresentation of pro-inflammatory bacteria—can increase intestinal permeability, promote systemic endotoxin exposure, and shift immune responses in ways that may exacerbate atopic dermatitis or inflammatory acne. Conversely, a balanced microbiome supports short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, immune tolerance, and epithelial repair, all of which help maintain healthier skin.

What a test shows: most consumer and clinical stool-based microbiome tests use sequencing to describe composition (which taxa are present and in what relative amounts) and diversity metrics. Some reports include functional inferences such as SCFA production potential, presence of lipopolysaccharide-producing organisms, or markers associated with fungal overgrowth. These signals can suggest interventions—dietary fiber to increase SCFAs, targeted probiotics known to modulate inflammation, or strategies to reduce foods that favor pathogenic taxa.

Evidence and limitations: several observational and interventional studies link gut composition with skin outcomes. For example, reduced bifidobacteria and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii have been associated with atopic dermatitis, and small intestinal bacterial overgrowth has been correlated with certain rosacea phenotypes. Randomized trials of probiotics show modest but meaningful effects on eczema incidence and severity in some populations. However, causality is complex: many studies are heterogeneous in design, and individual responses vary. A test provides data that should be interpreted alongside clinical history, diet, medications, and environmental factors.

Complementary testing: analyzing the skin microbiome alongside the gut can clarify whether skin-surface dysbiosis or systemic drivers are predominant. Skin swabs identify shifts in organisms such as Cutibacterium, Staphylococcus aureus, or Malassezia that may need topical or localized strategies in addition to gut-directed care.

Practical considerations: if test results indicate low microbial diversity or low SCFA-producing potential, evidence-based recommendations often include increasing dietary fiber and polyphenol-rich plants, addressing excessive antibiotic exposure, and considering probiotic strains with demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects.
