<p>Intestinal inflammation arises from diverse causes and has wide-ranging effects on digestion, nutrient absorption, and systemic health. This concise review summarizes the principal disease categories that produce intestinal inflammation, highlights the role of the gut microbiome in these processes, and points to diagnostic resources that can help clarify underlying drivers.</p>

<h2>Common infectious causes</h2>
<p>Bacterial, viral, and parasitic infections are frequent initiators of acute intestinal inflammation. Pathogens such as Salmonella, Campylobacter, pathogenic Escherichia coli, Clostridioides difficile, Norovirus, and Giardia directly damage the mucosa and provoke strong local immune responses. Even after clinical recovery, infections can leave persistent changes in microbial community structure that perpetuate low-grade inflammation and symptoms such as diarrhea, abdominal pain, and malabsorption.</p>

<h2>Chronic inflammatory conditions</h2>
<p>Inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD) — chiefly Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis — are autoimmune-mediated disorders characterized by sustained, often severe, intestinal inflammation visible on endoscopy and biopsy. IBD is associated with reduced microbial diversity and loss of anti-inflammatory taxa, and current research supports a bidirectional relationship between dysbiosis and immune dysregulation.</p>

<h2>Bowel disorders linked to dysbiosis</h2>
<p>Functional and motility-related disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) and small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) increasingly show evidence of low-grade inflammation and altered microbiomes. In IBS, shifts in bacterial composition and metabolic output can amplify nociception and bowel dysfunction. In SIBO, excessive bacterial density in the small intestine provokes inflammation, gas, and nutrient malabsorption.</p>

<h2>Autoimmune and non-infectious enteritis</h2>
<p>Autoimmune conditions (for example, celiac disease) and medication- or radiation-induced enteritis produce inflammatory damage without an infectious agent. These causes often coexist with or drive changes in the gut microbiome, complicating recovery and nutritional status.</p>

<h2>Mechanisms linking microbes and inflammation</h2>
<p>The gut microbiome influences mucosal immunity via microbial metabolites, barrier integrity, and direct immune signaling. Loss of short-chain fatty acid–producing bacteria (notably butyrate producers) undermines epithelial health, while expansion of endotoxin-producing Proteobacteria can stimulate innate immune activation. Lifestyle factors (diet, antibiotics, stress, sleep) further modulate these dynamics.</p>

<h2>Diagnostic and management considerations</h2>
<p>Identifying the specific disease processes behind intestinal inflammation is essential for targeted care. For clinicians and informed patients, microbial profiling can reveal dysbiosis patterns that support differential diagnosis and guide personalized interventions. For an overview of conditions and mechanisms, consult this [detailed guide on diseases that cause intestinal inflammation](https://www.innerbuddies.com/blogs/gut-health/what-diseases-cause-intestinal-inflammation).</p>
