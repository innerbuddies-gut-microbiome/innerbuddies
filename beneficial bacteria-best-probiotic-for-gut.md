# What is the best probiotic for the gut?

Probiotics are live microorganisms that can support digestion, immune function, and microbial balance. Determining the 'best' probiotic, however, requires evidence about which strains are most likely to address a specific person's gut profile. Microbiome testing converts stool DNA into actionable data, revealing deficiencies, overgrowths, and diversity metrics that guide targeted interventions.

Personalized approaches outperform one-size-fits-all supplementation because different strains have distinct functions. For example, Bifidobacterium species often help with carbohydrate fermentation and reducing bloating, Lactobacillus strains can assist lactose digestion and support mucosal health, and Saccharomyces boulardii is effective for preventing certain types of antibiotic-associated diarrhea. Choosing a probiotic that matches an identified need—rather than choosing by total CFU or marketing claims—improves the likelihood of meaningful benefit. Microbiome tests typically report which taxa are under- or over-represented, diversity scores, and markers linked to short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production. Low levels of SCFA producers such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii or Eubacterium spp. suggest the need for interventions that promote butyrate production—either via targeted probiotics, selected prebiotics, or diet. Conversely, identification of Proteobacteria overgrowth or pathogens can inform strategies that emphasize competitive strains and measures to reduce inflammation.

A precision-based strategy can be simple in practice: interpret the microbial profile, map deficits to known functional strains, and select probiotic formulations that include those strains at clinically supported dosages. For instance, when tests show depleted Lactobacilli after antibiotics, strains like L. rhamnosus GG or L. acidophilus are commonly recommended in the literature for recolonization. When mucosal integrity is of concern, species associated with barrier support (e.g., certain L. plantarum strains or Akkermansia-related interventions) become relevant targets.

Evidence also supports a role for targeted probiotics in gut–brain interactions. Specific combinations, including Bifidobacterium longum and Lactobacillus helveticus, have been studied for modest effects on stress-related symptoms, suggesting that microbiome-guided selection may have broader systemic benefits beyond digestion alone.
