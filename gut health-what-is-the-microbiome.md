The microbiome is the collective community of microorganisms and their genes that live on and inside the human body. Most attention centers on the gut microbiome—the dense ecosystem in the large intestine—because of its direct roles in digestion, immune regulation, and metabolic and neurological signaling. Understanding this microbial community helps explain how diet, medication, and lifestyle influence health and disease.

Microbial residents include bacteria, archaea, viruses (notably bacteriophages), fungi, and protozoa. These organisms perform complementary functions: breaking down complex carbohydrates and fibers into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) that nourish colon cells, synthesizing vitamins, modulating inflammation, and interacting with immune cells in the gut mucosa. Communication between microbes and host tissues—via metabolites, immune signals, and neural pathways—forms the basis of the gut–brain axis, which links intestinal ecology to mood and cognition.

When the microbiome is balanced, it contributes to digestion, pathogen resistance, and immune education. When balance is disrupted (a state often called dysbiosis), correlations emerge with conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease, metabolic disorders, certain autoimmune conditions, and mood disorders. These associations are supported by observational studies and mechanistic research in animals and humans, but causal pathways are complex and remain an active area of investigation.

Assessing the gut microbiome is increasingly accessible through stool-based sequencing tests that profile microbial composition and, in some cases, functional genes. Interpreting these results requires careful consideration of diversity metrics (richness and evenness), the presence or absence of keystone species, and signals of potential overgrowth or inflammation. Practical insights from testing can include whether fiber-digesting bacteria are abundant, whether SCFA producers are represented, or whether opportunistic organisms are present at unusual levels. Such information can inform diet adjustments (for example, increasing diverse plant fibers and polyphenol-rich foods), measured use of probiotics or prebiotics, and clinical follow-up when pathogenic signals appear. Microbial diversity is a key marker of resilience: higher diversity generally associates with greater functional redundancy and resistance to pathogen colonization. Factors that reduce diversity include broad-spectrum antibiotics, low-fiber diets, chronic stress, and certain environmental exposures. Conversely, diverse plant-based diets, fermented foods, and reduced unnecessary antibiotic use tend to support a richer microbiota.

When interpreted within context, microbial profiling can provide actionable signals to guide dietary and therapeutic choices and to prioritize follow-up with healthcare professionals.
