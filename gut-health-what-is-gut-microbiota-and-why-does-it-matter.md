Gut microbiota — often called gut flora — is the diverse community of bacteria, viruses, fungi and archaea that inhabit the human digestive tract. These microorganisms collectively influence digestion, immune function, nutrient production and metabolic processes. Research estimates trillions of microbes live in and on the human body, with a large proportion concentrated in the gut; their combined genomes (the microbiome) encode functions that human cells do not.

A balanced and diverse gut microbiota supports the gut barrier, synthesizes vitamins, ferments otherwise indigestible fibres into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) such as acetate, propionate and butyrate, and competes with pathogens to reduce infection risk. SCFAs serve as energy for colon cells, modulate inflammation and influence lipid and glucose metabolism in the liver. Conversely, reduced diversity or dysbiosis has been associated with digestive symptoms, altered immune responses and metabolic disturbances, though causal pathways remain an active area of study.

Multiple factors shape microbiota composition across the lifespan: mode of birth (vaginal vs. cesarean), breastfeeding, early antibiotic exposure, diet diversity, physical activity, environment (urban vs. rural) and pet exposure. Diet is among the most modifiable influences: fibre-rich foods and diverse plant-based substrates feed beneficial microbes and promote production of health-associated metabolites.
