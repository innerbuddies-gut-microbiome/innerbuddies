Digestive complaints are common and can stem from a range of causes: microbial imbalance, inflammation, structural issues, or immune-mediated reactions. This short guide explains the principal tests clinicians and researchers use to detect intestinal problems and what each method reveals. For a practical overview of testing options and how they fit into care, see this guide: [What tests are used to detect intestinal problems?](https://www.innerbuddies.com/blogs/gut-health/what-tests-are-used-to-detect-intestinal-problems).

Quick overview

- Stool microbiome testing evaluates microbial diversity and potential dysbiosis in the colon.
- Breath tests assess carbohydrate fermentation in the small intestine and help diagnose SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth).
- Fecal inflammatory markers such as calprotectin detect neutrophil-driven inflammation and help distinguish IBD from functional disorders.
- Food allergy/sensitivity panels and related immune tests identify reactions that may perpetuate gut symptoms.
- Endoscopy and imaging provide direct visualization of mucosal and structural pathology.

Key tests and what they show

1) Stool microbiome testing
Stool-based analyses use sequencing (16S rRNA or shotgun metagenomics) to profile bacteria, fungi, and viruses. Results report diversity metrics, relative abundances, and signatures that correlate with conditions such as IBS, IBD, or antibiotic-associated dysbiosis. These tests are non-invasive and increasingly available as at-home kits; laboratories translate sequencing data into clinically relevant patterns and possible intervention targets. An example resource for a consumer-facing option is <a href='https://www.innerbuddies.com/products/microbiome-test'>InnerBuddies microbiome test</a>.

2) Breath tests for SIBO
Hydrogen and methane breath tests measure gases produced when bacteria ferment test sugars (lactulose or glucose). Serial breath samples over 2–3 hours can reveal early rises in hydrogen or methane suggestive of bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine. Breath testing is non-invasive and useful when symptoms overlap with IBS.

3) Fecal calprotectin and other inflammatory markers
Calprotectin is a fecal protein released by neutrophils; elevated levels indicate intestinal inflammation and help differentiate inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis) from non-inflammatory conditions. Lactoferrin and stool occult blood tests are adjuncts used in workups for bleeding or mucosal injury.

4) Food sensitivity and allergy testing
Specific IgE tests identify immediate hypersensitivity (allergy), while IgG/IgA panels and elimination diets inform delayed reactions and intolerances. Results should be interpreted alongside clinical history because test specificity and clinical relevance vary.

5) Endoscopy and imaging
Upper endoscopy, colonoscopy, capsule endoscopy, abdominal ultrasound, CT, and MRI visualize mucosal and structural abnormalities (ulcers, strictures, tumors). Endoscopy can include biopsies for histology and microbial culture.

Practical considerations

Choice of test depends on symptoms, pretest probability, and the clinical question (infection, inflammation, structural vs. functional). Combining methods—stool analysis, breath testing, and imaging or endoscopy when indicated—improves diagnostic accuracy. Results should be reviewed with a clinician to align findings with symptoms and to plan evidence-based management.
