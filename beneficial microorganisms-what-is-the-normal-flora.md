The human body hosts a diverse community of microorganisms collectively known as the normal flora. Predominantly located in the gastrointestinal tract, these resident and commensal microbes—bacteria, archaea, viruses, and fungi—carry out metabolic, immune, and barrier functions that are foundational to health.

## Defining normal flora

Normal flora refers to the non-pathogenic microorganisms that live on and in the body without causing disease under typical conditions. In the gut, resident microbiota such as members of the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes establish early in life and remain relatively stable, while transient microbes appear intermittently through diet or environment. ## Core functions and symbiosis

Microbial symbiosis in the gut is mutually beneficial: the host supplies nutrients and habitat, and microbes perform tasks the host cannot. Key activities include fermentation of dietary fiber into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate, synthesis of certain vitamins (e.g., K and some B vitamins), enhancement of mineral absorption, and modulation of local and systemic immune responses. Commensal species contribute to colonization resistance by occupying ecological niches and limiting pathogen overgrowth.

## Testing the gut ecosystem

## Clinical and health relevance

Alterations in the normal flora—dysbiosis—are associated with conditions ranging from inflammatory bowel disease and metabolic disorders to immune dysregulation and mood changes. Evidence supports relationships between reduced microbial diversity and increased inflammatory markers, though causality is complex and often bidirectional. Interventions with dietary fiber, fermented foods, prebiotics, and targeted probiotics can shift microbial composition, but responses vary by individual.

## Conserving the normal flora

Strategies to support a resilient microbiome focus on consistent dietary patterns rich in diverse fibers and polyphenols, prudent antibiotic stewardship, regular physical activity, adequate sleep, and stress management. Longitudinal stability of resident microbes is reinforced by these lifestyle practices, which help maintain metabolic outputs such as SCFAs and barrier integrity.

## Looking ahead
