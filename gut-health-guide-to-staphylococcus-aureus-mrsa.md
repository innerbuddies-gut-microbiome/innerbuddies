Staphylococcus aureus is a common Gram-positive bacterium that lives on the skin and in the nose of many healthy people. While most strains are harmless colonizers, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) carries resistance to methicillin and related β-lactam antibiotics, making some infections harder to treat. What MRSA is and how it differs

MRSA harbors the mecA gene, which alters penicillin-binding proteins and reduces susceptibility to many common antibiotics. In contrast, methicillin-sensitive S. aureus (MSSA) remains susceptible to standard β-lactams. Clinically, MRSA appears both as community-acquired (CA-MRSA), often producing rapid skin abscesses, and healthcare-associated (HA-MRSA), which can cause severe bloodstream infections and pneumonia.

Transmission and risk settings

MRSA spreads primarily via direct skin-to-skin contact and indirectly via contaminated objects such as towels, sports equipment, or medical devices. High-risk settings include hospitals, long-term care facilities, athletic facilities, prisons, and crowded housing. Routine hand hygiene and environmental cleaning are foundational to reducing spread. Clinical impact and statistics

Serious S. aureus infections include skin abscesses, cellulitis, surgical-wound infections, bloodstream infection (bacteraemia), pneumonia, and toxin-mediated syndromes. In 2017, the U.S. recorded approximately 119,000 bloodstream Staph infections with nearly 20,000 associated deaths; reductions in hospital MRSA rates were observed until the mid-2010s but progress has slowed. In England (2022–23) MRSA bacteraemia incidence was reported at about 1 per 100,000 people, with many cases originating outside hospitals.

Diagnosis and laboratory testing

Diagnosis commonly relies on culture with antibiotic susceptibility testing (MIC), supplemented by rapid molecular assays (PCR) in many clinical laboratories. Identifying colonization sites (nares, skin) can guide decolonization strategies in selected contexts.

Treatment principles

Treatment depends on severity and susceptibility. Mild, localized skin infections are managed with incision and drainage; topical or oral antibiotics may be used based on sensitivity. Serious invasive infections often require intravenous agents such as vancomycin, daptomycin, or linezolid. Oral alternatives for certain infections include trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, clindamycin, or doxycycline when appropriate.

Prevention and stewardship

Effective prevention includes consistent hand hygiene, barrier precautions in care settings, disinfection of shared surfaces, and targeted decolonization (e.g., nasal mupirocin and chlorhexidine washes) when indicated. Antibiotic stewardship—appropriate selection, dosing, and duration of therapy—is essential to limit further resistance.

Research and outlook

Ongoing research explores vaccines, bacteriophage therapy, improved rapid diagnostics, and strategies to support beneficial microbiome interactions that may suppress S. aureus overgrowth. Conclusion

MRSA remains a significant public health concern, but a combination of hygiene practices, surveillance, targeted treatment, and stewardship can reduce its impact. Understanding transmission, timely diagnosis, and evidence-based prevention are key to protecting individuals and communities.
