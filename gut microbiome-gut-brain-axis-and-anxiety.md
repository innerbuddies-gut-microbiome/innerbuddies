The gut‑brain axis describes the dynamic, two‑way communication network linking the gastrointestinal tract and the central nervous system. This network—mediated by neural pathways (including the vagus nerve), immune signaling, endocrine factors, and microbial metabolites—helps regulate mood, cognition, and stress responses. Recent research indicates that disruptions in gut ecology can influence anxiety-related processes via changes in neurotransmitter availability, neuroinflammation, and barrier integrity.

Microbial communities in the gut synthesize or modulate precursors for key neurochemicals such as serotonin, gamma‑aminobutyric acid (GABA), and dopamine. Alterations in microbial composition (dysbiosis) have been associated with reduced production of short‑chain fatty acids like butyrate, increased systemic inflammatory markers, and changes in intestinal permeability—factors that can affect brain function. Population and animal studies have observed differences in bacterial taxa between individuals with anxiety disorders and control groups. Beneficial genera such as Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, and Faecalibacterium are frequently underrepresented in anxious cohorts, while increases in proinflammatory or endotoxin‑producing groups (for example, certain Proteobacteria) correlate with heightened inflammatory tone. These patterns are hypothesis‑generating rather than definitive: human microbiome studies are heterogeneous in methods, and causality remains an active area of investigation.

Microbiome testing can provide a personalized snapshot of gut community structure and functional potential. Tests that report diversity indices, relative abundances, and inferred metabolic outputs may help clinicians and researchers identify targets for intervention. Mechanistically, neuroinflammation is a plausible mediator between gut disturbances and anxiety. Loss of barrier integrity ('leaky gut') and elevated lipopolysaccharide (LPS) exposure can promote systemic cytokine release and microglial activation, which in turn influence neurotransmitter systems and synaptic plasticity. Stress physiology also interacts with the microbiome: activation of the hypothalamic‑pituitary‑adrenal (HPA) axis alters microbial composition and gut permeability, while certain microbes can modulate HPA signaling in animal models.

Interventions under study include dietary modification (fiber, polyphenols, fermented foods), prebiotics, probiotics with psychotropic potential, and behavioral approaches that reduce stress. Interpretation of microbiome data should be done in the context of clinical symptoms, comorbidities, medications, and diet.
