Gut health underpins digestion, immune function, metabolism, and even mood. Testing provides objective data to supplement symptoms like bloating, irregular bowel movements, fatigue, or skin issues. A structured testing approach combines assessments of digestive function, microbial composition, inflammation, and intestinal permeability to create a clearer clinical picture.

Understanding what to test

Begin with symptom mapping and basic labs. Tests commonly used include hydrogen/methane breath tests to detect SIBO, stool enzyme analyses for pancreatic function, and gastric pH evaluation for acid-related disorders. For microbial composition and functional potential, modern sequencing methods give the most insight. Microbiome analysis

Stool DNA sequencing (16S rRNA or whole-genome shotgun) identifies which organisms are present and estimates diversity, a marker of resilience. Whole-genome methods additionally infer metabolic capacity—such as short-chain fatty acid production—that influences gut lining health and systemic effects. Culture-based methods and PCR panels can complement sequencing when specific pathogens are suspected. Inflammation and stool biomarkers

Noninvasive stool markers like calprotectin and lactoferrin detect intestinal inflammation and help distinguish inflammatory bowel disease from functional conditions. Secretory IgA indicates mucosal immune activity. Measuring short-chain fatty acids and markers of microbial metabolism can clarify whether dysbiosis is contributing to symptoms. Combining inflammation markers with sequencing results guides appropriate timing of interventions and reduces the risk of exacerbating active inflammation.

Intestinal permeability

Tests such as the lactulose-mannitol challenge and zonulin measurements evaluate barrier integrity. Increased permeability is implicated in food sensitivities, systemic inflammation, and some autoimmune conditions. If permeability is elevated, therapeutic focus often shifts to removing triggers and supporting mucosal repair before aggressive microbial-modifying therapies.

Choosing stool testing methods

Comprehensive stool analyses, PCR pathogen panels, and DNA-based microbiome tests each have strengths. PCR and sequencing offer greater sensitivity for low-abundance organisms; culture methods may help isolate organisms for targeted treatment. When selecting tests, consider clinical questions, cost, and whether follow-up interpretation by a clinician or nutrition specialist is available. Interpreting results and next steps

Test results should be integrated with clinical history. High inflammation may warrant diagnostic referral; dysbiosis or low diversity points to targeted dietary and microbial strategies. Lab-driven plans commonly include dietary fiber optimization, selective probiotics or prebiotics informed by results, and agents to support barrier repair.
